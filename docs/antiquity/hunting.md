# Hunting & Wild Animals

<p class="bb-lead">In vanilla, a cow stands still and waits to be punched. Not here. In Antiquity the wild has nerves: prey watches you, bolts when you get close, drags the whole herd with it when it panics, and tires only if you have the legs to run it down. Learn to read that fear — to sneak, to lure, to wound and track — and the hunt becomes a craft in its own right.</p>

!!! abstract "The short version"
    - **Wild prey flees a player it can see.** It bolts from about **24 blocks** away — **half that** if you sneak, and **farther** if you sprint. Breaking blocks is noisy and gives you away.
    - **Hold an animal's favourite food** and it stops fearing you. **Feed** it once and it's **tamed** for good — calm vanilla livestock from then on.
    - **Spook one animal and the whole herd scatters** — the alarm jumps to every same-species animal nearby.
    - **A long chase tires prey.** A winded animal slows down, so a determined hunter can **run it down** — cows especially.
    - **A thrown spear opens a bleeding wound** that leaves a **blood trail** on the ground you can follow. Many animals also leave **footprint tracks**, and the **Hunting Instincts** research lights up a whole trail at a glance.
    - **A spooked boar may charge** instead of flee, and **wild wolves and ocelots are hostile** — the early wild bites back.

!!! tip "This is how you *and* your hunters hunt"
    Everything on this page governs wild animals for **you**, the player. Your settlement's **[Hunter](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md#hunter)** citizens live by the exact same rules — they crouch-stalk, spook herds, and chase tiring quarry just as you do. Understanding wild behaviour first-hand makes you a better master of the hunt.

## Wild things are wary

Left alone, a wild animal grazes and wanders like always. But the moment a player it can **see** comes within range, it **flees** — turning and running for cover, and staying skittish for a while after. How close you can get before it bolts depends entirely on how much noise you're making:

| What you're doing | It notices you from about |
|---|---|
| **Sneaking** (crouched) | 12 blocks |
| Standing or walking | 24 blocks |
| **Sprinting** | 36 blocks |

So the golden rule of the approach is **crouch to close the distance**. Sneak in, and you can halve how near you have to be before the animal panics. Sprint, and your own footfalls carry half again as far — fine for the chase, fatal for the stalk.

Two more things shape the approach:

- **Line of sight matters.** By default an animal only flees a player it can actually *see*. Put a hill, a treeline, or a wall between you and your quarry and you can move up unnoticed — then break cover for the shot.
- **Digging gives you away.** Breaking a block is loud. Any prey within about **16 blocks** is spooked by the noise, even if it never saw you. Don't mine, chop, or quarry next to the herd you're trying to sneak up on.

!!! note "How long the fear lasts"
    Once spooked, an animal stays alarmed for several seconds and keeps fleeing the whole time you give chase — every stride you take toward it re-arms its fear. Break off, let it calm down, and it drifts back to grazing.

## Luring — and taming for good

Fear has an off switch: **an animal's favourite food.** Hold the food it likes — wheat for cattle, seeds for chickens, carrots for pigs, and so on — in either hand, and it will **not** flee you (and a pig won't charge). You can walk straight up to a lured animal. This is the quiet counterpart to the chase: instead of running your quarry down, you coax it in.

**Feed** a wild livestock animal its favourite food and you do more than lure it — you **tame** it. From that moment it reverts to ordinary, calm vanilla behaviour: it no longer flees, leaves no footprints, and is immune to hunting fear and bleeding. A puff of hearts marks the change, and it **sticks forever** — that animal is settled livestock now, not wild game.

!!! tip "Which animals this works on"
    Food-taming only applies to the **untameable livestock** — cows, sheep, pigs, chickens, and their kin. Animals with their *own* vanilla taming (**wolves, cats, horses**) keep those rules instead. And any animal your settlement's **[Herder](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md#herder)** corrals and raises counts as domesticated automatically — a penned herd stays calm even when wild animals around it are bolting.

## Spooking the herd

Panic is contagious. The instant one animal takes fright — it spots you, takes a hit, or hears you dig — it **alarms every same-species animal within about 12 blocks.** Those herd-mates bolt too, scattering to cover *even if they never saw you themselves*. One careless step at the edge of a meadow can empty the whole field in seconds.

The lesson for the hunter is patience: **line up a clean first strike before the alarm spreads.** Approach from cover, pick your animal, and drop it (or wound it) before its neighbours know you're there — because the moment the herd scatters, you're chasing singles across open ground.

## Running it down

An animal doesn't have endless wind. It burns **stamina** only while it's actively fleeing you, and recovers it when calm. Push a chase long enough and your quarry **tires** — a winded animal slows noticeably, and a hunter with the legs for it can eventually **run the animal down** on foot. This is the oldest hunting technique there is: not outsprinting your prey, but outlasting it.

How catchable an animal is depends on its species:

- **Cows are the slowest** — sluggish even at a full run. You can chase down a cow on foot without much trouble.
- **Rabbits and foxes are the fastest**, and will leave a walking player far behind until they tire.
- **Most other animals** outpace a walking player but not a sprinting, persistent one — wear them out and they're yours.

!!! tip "Stack the odds"
    A **wounded** animal (see below) also runs slower, and a **tired** wounded animal slower still. Open with a spear, then keep the pressure on — the chase gets easier the longer it goes.

## The bleeding wound & blood trail

Land a **thrown spear** in an animal and you open a **bleeding wound.** (A melee poke or an arrow won't — it takes a spear sunk into flesh.) For a while afterward the wound pulses: the animal takes a trickle of damage every few seconds, sprays a burst of blood, and runs a little slower for being hurt.

Crucially, **each pulse leaves a splash of blood on the ground**, and those splashes linger for about a minute before fading. The result is a **blood trail** — a line of red splats leading straight to your fleeing, bleeding quarry. Throw your spear, let the animal run, and follow the blood to wherever it drops or tires. A wounded animal that escapes your sight is rarely lost; it's just at the end of a trail.

!!! note "Poison is a different wound"
    A **blowdart** doesn't bleed — it poisons, which behaves quite differently. Some poisons even *stop* an animal fleeing instead of making it run: **Wolfsbane** roots prey in place so you can walk up and make the kill. That whole toolkit lives on the **[Herbalism](herbalism.md)** page.

## Reading tracks

Many animals press **footprint tracks** into the ground as they move — cattle, sheep, pigs, chickens, wolves, and horses all leave prints. A few details make the tracks worth reading:

- An animal only leaves prints while **walking** — a standing animal leaves none.
- A **sprinting** animal leaves *far fewer*, its strides stretched wide apart, so a sparse, spaced-out trail means something ran through here fast.
- Tracks **fade after a minute or two**, so a fresh, crisp trail is a recent one.
- A **tamed** or domesticated animal leaves no tracks at all.

**Right-click a track to examine it.** It lights up and shows a **direction cone** pointing the way the animal was heading — enough to tell you which way your quarry went. Research **Hunting Instincts** and the examine does much more: it picks out **every other track left by that same animal** and tints them **cyan** for about ten seconds, so a jumble of prints on the ground resolves into one clear, continuous trail you can simply walk to its maker.

!!! tip "Blood and prints together"
    On a real hunt the two systems work as one: the **blood trail** points you to a wounded animal in the short term, and **footprint tracks** — especially with Hunting Instincts lit — carry the trail across ground where the blood has already faded.

## The boar's charge

A cornered pig doesn't always run. When a pig is spooked, roughly **half the time** it turns and **charges** instead of fleeing — and only *one* pig in a herd will do so at a time, while the rest scatter as normal. The charge comes in three beats you can learn to read:

1. **Wind-up** — the boar squares up on you and pauses for a heartbeat. This is your tell.
2. **Charge** — it dashes forward along its look, tracking you *slightly* so a standing target gets clipped but a sharp **sidestep** dodges it clean.
3. **Impact** — connect, and it's a heavy hit, around **three hearts**. Then even the charger breaks and flees like the rest.

Piglets don't charge — only grown boars. Keep moving, watch for the square-up, and dodge across the charge line rather than backpedalling down it.

## Dangerous predators

Not everything on four legs is prey. **Wild wolves and ocelots are hostile** — they hunt players and working hunters on sight, and they're **tougher than their vanilla cousins**: faster on their feet and hitting noticeably harder. A wolf pack is a real threat in the early days, when you're still swinging bone and flint. Two things call them off:

- **Hold a bone** and a wild wolf reads it as taming intent and leaves you be — your opening to tame it the ordinary way.
- **Hold a predator's food** and it won't attack, exactly as food lulls prey.

Tamed or owned wolves stay friendly, as always. Ocelots are worth a special note: vanilla ocelots *flee* the player, but Antiquity strips that instinct out — so a wild cat here comes at you instead of skittering away.

!!! warning "Arm yourself before you wander"
    Because the wild fights back, a stone-age hunter shouldn't roam far unarmed. Carry a spear at minimum, and a **[bow](fletching-and-archery.md)** if you have one — the same weapons that take game also turn back a wolf. Better weapons make cleaner kills, too, which matters at the [Tannery](tannery.md#how-hide-quality-is-decided).

## It's all under your control

Every behaviour on this page — the fleeing, the herd alarm, persistence stamina, bleeding, blood splats, footprints, the boar charge, and predator hostility — is an individual **Antiquity Config** toggle, and they all ship **switched on**. On a default world, the wild is exactly this wary.

!!! note "For server owners"
    The `hunting` section of the Antiquity config exposes each knob — flee ranges, the herd-alarm radius, mining-noise radius, boar-charge chance, bleed timing, footprint frequency, predator hostility, and more — plus a master switch. Turn the master toggle off and animals revert to plain vanilla behaviour; tune the individual values to make the hunt easier or harder to taste. The numbers quoted on this page are the defaults.
