# Metalworking & Bronze

<p class="bb-lead"><em>No electric furnace, no anvil press — just mud, coal, a bellows and your own timing. This is the flagship craft of the Antiquity age: draw metal out of stone, pour it into moulds you shaped yourself, and hammer it into tools that carry your people toward iron.</em></p>

!!! abstract "The pipeline, end to end"
    Metalworking runs in five moves. Learn the shape of it first, then master each stage below.

    1. **Smelt** — Build a **Bloomery** from Mud Bricks and coal. It smelts raw copper and iron straight into ingots.
    2. **Melt** — Charge a fired-clay **Crucible** with raw ore (50 mB a piece), set it in a lit Bloomery, and let it turn liquid.
    3. **Cast** — Dress a **Stone Anvil**, set a fired **mould** on it, and pour the molten crucible to cast a rough head or blade.
    4. **Forge** — Stack the cast part on the anvil and **cold-hammer** it. A timing minigame sets the tool's **quality**.
    5. **Alloy** — Melt **copper + tin** together in the crucible to make **bronze** — harder, longer-lasting, the proving ground before iron.

Everything here is hand-work. Once you have the rhythm, the **Smith** crafter can run the whole chain for you in a [Smithy](#the-smith-and-the-smithy).

## The Bloomery — your first furnace

The **Bloomery** is the heart of Antiquity smelting: a squat mud-brick stack that burns hot enough to free metal from ore. Everything downstream — melting, casting, annealing — needs a lit one.

### Building it

Stack **two Mud Bricks** where you want the furnace, then hold a **Block of Coal** and right-click the *lower* block. The two bricks and the coal fold together into a finished Bloomery, complete with a swinging door.

- [x] Stack 2 Mud Bricks vertically
- [x] Hold a Block of Coal
- [x] Right-click the lower Mud Bricks block

!!! tip "Research first"
    Smelting is gated behind the **Smelting** research — queue it at your settlement Campfire. Reaching it (and later Iron Working) is a genuine turning point; see [Research & Eras](../civilization/research-and-eras.md).

### Loading and lighting

**Shift + right-click** swings the door open. With the door open, **right-click with ingredients** to load them inside — raw ore, or a charged crucible (more on that below).

Then light it. Hold **Fire Sticks** (or a Flint & Steel) aimed at the open furnace for a couple of seconds until it catches. The flames won't last on their own — **they die down after a while**. To keep the heat up, hold a **Bellows** aimed at the Bloomery and keep pumping. That steady breath of air is what carries a long smelt to the finish.

!!! warning "The fire is not set-and-forget"
    Walk away and the flames sink toward *No Fire*. For anything longer than a quick copper smelt, stay on the bellows — or you'll come back to a cold furnace and an unfinished melt.

### Reading the heat

The Bloomery shows a live **temperature readout** in °C, plus a plain-language band telling you where you stand. Watch the band, not the exact number:

| Band | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| **No Fire** | Unlit or gone out — nothing is happening. Light it or hit the bellows. |
| **Too Low** | Warming, but not hot enough to work. Keep pumping. |
| **Okay** | In range and making slow progress. |
| **Good** | The sweet spot — smelting and melting move fastest here. |
| **Too High** | Overshot. Ease off the bellows and let it settle. |

Aim to hold the reading in **Good**. Each metal turns liquid at its own temperature, so a hotter melt is what unlocks the tougher alloys:

| Metal | Turns liquid at |
| --- | --- |
| Tin | 232 °C |
| Bronze | 950 °C |
| Copper | 1085 °C |

### Smelting ore into ingots

Loaded straight with raw ore, the Bloomery works as a plain smelter — it turns **raw copper** and **raw iron** into ingots (and a raw iron *block* into an iron block, given a long, patient burn). Copper comes quickly; iron takes noticeably longer and asks more of your bellows arm.

When the smelt finishes, **open the door and right-click empty-handed** to take the output.

!!! info "Two roads for copper"
    Raw copper can go either way: smelt it to a solid **ingot** here, or melt it in a **crucible** (next section) as liquid metal for casting. Iron is smelted to ingots for later, Medieval-era toolmaking — it is not cast into bronze-age parts. Tin is never smelted to an ingot in the Bloomery at all — it only melts in a **crucible**, chiefly on its way to bronze.

## The Crucible — melting for the pour

Casting needs *liquid* metal, and the vessel that holds it is the **Crucible**. It starts as clay: shape an Unfired Crucible at the Pottery Slab and harden it in a **Kiln** — the same firing you use for bricks and moulds. See [Pottery](pottery.md) for the clay-and-kiln side of things.

**Charging it** is simple:

- **Right-click with raw ore** to drop a piece in. Each piece adds **50 mB** of metal, up to **8 pieces** (400 mB) in one crucible.
- **Right-click empty-handed** to pop the *last* piece back out if you overfilled or grabbed the wrong ore.

A charged crucible is still just cold ore in a cup. To melt it, **place the crucible inside a lit Bloomery** and hold the heat in the working band until the whole charge goes liquid. The Bloomery's readout switches to show the molten metal and how many mB you have. Now it's a **molten crucible** — pick it up and carry it, glowing, to the anvil.

!!! warning "Molten metal cools"
    A crucible left sitting too long will cool and set back toward solid. If it goes cold, just **reheat it in a Bloomery** — no metal is lost, but you can't pour a crucible that has gone hard.

## Moulds — shaping what you'll cast

You don't pour metal into thin air — you pour it into a **fired clay mould** cut to the shape of the part you want. Making one is a small craft of its own:

1. Make a **Clay Mold Base** at the Pottery Slab.
2. **Right-click the base with a wood or stone tool of the shape you want** — a Wooden Axe imprints an axe mould, a stone pickaxe imprints a pickaxe mould, and so on. The base takes that tool's silhouette.
3. **Fire the imprinted mould in a Kiln** to harden it into a reusable **fired mould**.

There is a mould for every part in the age. Each one drinks a set amount of metal when you pour — plan your crucible charge around it (remember: **50 mB per ore piece**).

| Mould | Metal needed | Min. ore (50 mB each) | Left in crucible |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Chisel | 60 mB | 2 pieces (100 mB) | 40 mB |
| Arrow | 60 mB | 2 pieces (100 mB) | 40 mB |
| Knife | 80 mB | 2 pieces (100 mB) | 20 mB |
| Hoe | 100 mB | 2 pieces (100 mB) | — |
| Shovel | 100 mB | 2 pieces (100 mB) | — |
| Ingot | 100 mB | 2 pieces (100 mB) | — |
| Spear | 120 mB | 3 pieces (150 mB) | 30 mB |
| Axe | 150 mB | 3 pieces (150 mB) | — |
| Pickaxe | 150 mB | 3 pieces (150 mB) | — |
| Hammer | 180 mB | 4 pieces (200 mB) | 20 mB |
| Sword | 200 mB | 4 pieces (200 mB) | — |

!!! tip "Pour only takes what it needs"
    A pour fills the mould up to its requirement and **no further** — any extra metal stays in the crucible to cool, top up a second cast, or add to your next charge. A single well-filled crucible can often cast two small parts back to back.

## Casting at the Stone Anvil

The **Stone Anvil** is where liquid becomes solid on purpose. It's both your casting bed and your forge.

### Dressing the anvil

You don't craft a Stone Anvil in a grid — you *dress* one. **Right-click a plain Stone block with a Hammer** and it's shaped into a Stone Anvil in place. (That first Hammer is itself cast metal — a Stone Hammer Head knapped and hafted early gets you started; see [Tool Tiers](tool-tiers.md).)

### Setting a mould and pouring

1. **Set a fired mould on the empty anvil.** This tells the anvil what you intend to cast — the axe mould means you're casting an axe head, the sword mould a blade, and so on.
2. **Hold right-click with a molten crucible** aimed at the mould. The metal pours in, filling to the mould's requirement.
3. **Wait for it to cool and set.** Molten metal doesn't harden instantly.
4. **Right-click empty-handed** to lift the finished **casting** — a rough head or blade — off the anvil.

!!! failure "Not enough metal"
    If your crucible can't fill the mould, the cast fails and the metal is wasted — the readout will warn you the casting was underfilled. Check the mould's requirement against your crucible's mB *before* you pour. A sword mould wants 200 mB; a crucible with only three ore pieces (150 mB) won't complete it.

## Cold-hammer forging

A fresh casting is blunt and rough — a head, not yet a tool. You finish it **cold**, on the same anvil, with a Hammer and a steady hand.

**Set the cast part on the Stone Anvil together with a stick** for the haft, hold a **Hammer**, and right-click to begin. A cursor sweeps across a band with a **green** target zone. The on-screen hint says it plainly:

> Drag down to swing — release on the green before it shrinks.

Land your release **inside the green** for a clean strike. Every clean hit **work-hardens** the metal, and the green zone **narrows and speeds up** — each strike is harder to nail than the last. Each part needs a set number of clean strikes to finish: a knife takes only a couple, an axe a few, a sword more. String enough good strikes together and the tool's quality climbs:

| Quality | Feel |
| --- | --- |
| **Crude** | Rushed or unlucky work — the floor. |
| **Standard** | Solid, dependable. |
| **Fine** | Well-made, noticeably better. |
| **Masterwork** | The peak — top durability, effectiveness, and a shot at a special perk. |

Quality drives the tool's **durability**, its **effectiveness**, and a **chance at a special perk**, so it's well worth chasing.

!!! tip "Bank early, push carefully"
    **Sneak + right-click** to stop and **bank the quality you've earned** — a safe exit. The temptation is always to push one more strike for the next tier. Weigh it: a miss is costly.

!!! warning "Miss on hardened metal and it cracks"
    Once the metal is work-hardened, a **missed strike cracks it and drops a tier**. If a piece is getting brittle and the band feels impossibly tight, don't gamble — carry it to a **Bloomery** and hold the **anneal** band. Annealing resets the metal to *wide-and-slow* **without losing the hardness you already earned**, so you can keep climbing safely. Careful smiths anneal between pushes.

!!! example "A copper axe, start to finish"
    1. **Smelt or gather** raw copper. Charge a crucible with **3 pieces** (150 mB) — exactly an axe head.
    2. **Melt** the crucible in a lit Bloomery, held in the *Good* band, until it reads molten.
    3. **Set an Axe Mold** on your dressed Stone Anvil and **pour** the molten crucible in.
    4. **Wait**, then lift the rough **Copper Axe Head**.
    5. **Stack the head plus a stick** on the anvil, take up a Hammer, and **cold-hammer** — release on the green, sneak-click to bank once you're happy. Out comes a finished **Copper Axe** at the quality you forged.

## Bronze — the alloy

**Bronze is not an ore.** There is no bronze vein to mine — you *make* it, in the melt, by alloying two metals.

Charge a single crucible with **both copper and tin** and melt them together in the Bloomery. The proportions are what matter:

- Keep **copper between 60% and 90%** of the charge.
- Keep **tin between 10% and 40%**.

Hit that band and the whole melt resolves cleanly to **Molten Bronze**. Stray outside it — too little tin, too much, or a third metal in the pot — and the charge falls back to a poorer, off-ratio result. To help you dial it in, the Bloomery readout shows the **running composition** and a **✓ / ✗ ratio check** as you add ore, so you can see the moment your mix crosses into a clean bronze.

Bronze pours into the **same moulds** as copper and tin, but casts a **harder, longer-lasting** tool. It is the backbone of the age — clearly better than copper, and the workhorse metal right up until you unlock iron.

!!! tip "A workable starting mix"
    Roughly **three parts copper to one part tin** sits comfortably inside the valid band. From an 8-piece crucible, that's about **6 copper + 2 tin** (400 mB of bronze) — enough for two axe heads or a sword blade. Watch the ✓ before you commit the melt.

!!! note "Tin's place in the chain"
    Tin melts at the lowest heat of any metal here, so it's easy to work — but its real value is as the junior partner that marries copper into **bronze**. You *can* cast tin parts on their own, yet the reason you prospect for tin is the alloy. Go looking for it once you're ready to move past pure copper. (For how the metals compare as finished tools, see [Tool Tiers](tool-tiers.md).)

## The Smith and the Smithy

Everything above is something *you* can do by hand — but a growing settlement shouldn't have you camped at the anvil forever. The **Smith** is the crafter who takes it all over: they **work the Bloomery, cast moulds, and cold-hammer metal tools** on your behalf, inside a **Smithy** workshop.

Stand up a Smithy, assign a citizen to the Smith job, and your metal chain becomes part of the settlement's economy rather than a solo grind. See [Workshops](../civilization/workshops.md) for laying one out and [Jobs & Labor](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md) for staffing it.

!!! quote "Why it all matters"
    Copper opens the door; bronze walks you through it. Iron Working is the capstone that carries your people into the Medieval era — but every iron age is built on the mud-brick furnaces and hand-forged bronze that came first.
