# Cooking & Food

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Raw meat rots. A warm stew feeds a whole tribe. Learn to cook, salt, and dry your food, and you turn a lucky hunt into a larder that carries your people through the leaner days.</em></p>

!!! abstract "The short of it"
    - Fresh food slowly turns **Bland** (worth half as much), then rots into worthless **Spoiled Food**.
    - **Salt** a perishable to make it keep about **twice as long** (roughly +100%); a **Drying Rack** turns meat and fish into dried rations that *never* spoil.
    - A **Stone Cooking Pot** over a lit campfire boils raw food into a **Stew** — a big, rot-proof pile of stored food that fights starvation better than anything else this age.
    - Only *good* food counts. Spoiled or poisoned food adds **nothing** to your settlement's [food supply](../civilization/food.md).

Everything here builds on the [food economy](../civilization/food.md): your fishers, farmers, and herders keep raw food flowing, and the tricks on this page keep that food from going to waste before your citizens can eat it.

---

## Why food spoils

Every perishable food in Bannerbound carries a **freshness**, shown right in its tooltip. Left alone, it walks downhill:

| Stage | Tooltip | What it means |
| --- | --- | --- |
| :material-leaf: Fresh | **Fresh** | Full food value — freshly caught, harvested, or cooked. |
| :material-clock-alert-outline: Bland | **Bland (½ food value)** | Past its prime. Still edible, but worth only **half** what it was. |
| :material-delete-variant: Spoiled | *Spoiled Food* | Rotted through. A worthless item that feeds no one. |

Spoilage is a slow, quiet gamble rather than a ticking clock. Freshness is a simple label on the stack — not a hidden per-item timer — so your food still stacks together neatly. As the days pass, each stack rolls a small, steady chance to slip down a stage, so you can never pin the exact moment it turns. Different foods last different lengths of time: cooked meat keeps far longer than a raw cut, and a raw fish is among the first things in your pack to turn.

!!! warning "Rot doesn't stop in the chest"
    Food spoils whether it's in your pack, dropped on the ground, or sitting in your settlement's claimed storage. Stockpiling raw meat and forgetting about it just fills your larder with **Spoiled Food**. Preserve it, cook it, or eat it.

The whole point of this page is to slow that slide — or stop it cold.

## The Stone Cooking Pot

The **Stone Cooking Pot** is your first real kitchen: a heavy stone cauldron you fill with water, set over a fire, and simmer raw food into a hearty **Stew**.

!!! info "Unlock it first"
    The cooking pot arrives with the **Stews** research, an Ancient-era study that follows [Food Preservation](#salt-and-preservation) and Spear Fishing on the [research tree](../civilization/research-and-eras.md). Until you've learned it, you can't carve the pot.

**Craft** the pot on a [Crafting Stone](knapping.md):

| Ingredients | Result |
| --- | --- |
| 5 × Cobblestone + 1 × Stick | 1 × Stone Cooking Pot |

### Cooking, step by step

- [ ] **Fill it with water.** Hold the pot and right-click a water source to fill it before you place it, or set the empty pot down and right-click it with a water bucket.
- [ ] **Set it over fire.** Place the filled pot directly **on top of a lit campfire**. The pot settles down onto the flames, and the fire's glow tells you it's heating.
- [ ] **Add your food.** Right-click the pot with food to drop ingredients in — up to **8 items** in one pot. The water takes on the colour of what's cooking.
- [ ] **Wait for the simmer.** Bubbles rise and the broth darkens as it cooks. Give it a short while over the fire.
- [ ] **Eat.** Right-click the finished pot **empty-handed** to scoop out a serving. Each pot holds roughly **six servings**.

!!! warning "Common mistakes"
    - The pot only cooks over a **normal lit campfire** — it will **not** cook on your town hall's fire.
    - Adding a new ingredient **restarts the simmer**, so drop in *everything* you want first, then let it cook undisturbed.
    - Let the fire go out and the pot's progress slowly slips backward. Keep the campfire lit until the stew is done.

!!! example "A pot of beef stew"
    You come home from a hunt with raw beef and a few potatoes. Cook the beef on a campfire first, then carve a Stone Cooking Pot, fill it at the river, and set it on a second lit campfire. Right-click in **4 cooked beef** and **4 potatoes**, wait out the simmer, and you're left with a steaming **Beef Stew** — six servings of rot-proof food your whole camp can share.

## Stews

A finished stew is the best food store of the Stone Age. It **never spoils**, and its food value drains slowly as it's eaten — by you, by wandering citizens, and by your settlement's larder — until the pot runs dry and empties back to bare stone. A single well-stocked pot is a **strong, stable [food surplus](../civilization/food.md)** and your surest defense against a [Starvation crisis](../civilization/crises.md).

The pot recognises certain pairings as **named stews**. Add *exactly* the ingredients a recipe calls for — nothing more, nothing less — to brew it:

| Stew | What to add |
| --- | --- |
| **Beef Stew** | Cooked Beef + Potato |
| **Chicken Stew** | Cooked Chicken + Carrot |
| **Mutton Stew** | Cooked Mutton + Carrot |
| **Rabbit Stew** | Cooked Rabbit + Carrot + Brown Mushroom |
| **Mushroom Stew** | Red Mushroom + Brown Mushroom |
| **Beetroot Stew** | Beetroot |
| **Potato Stew** | Potato |
| **Vegetable Stew** | Any mix of Carrot, Potato, and Beetroot |
| **Stew** *(generic)* | Any other edible mixture |

Any combination the recipes don't recognise still cooks — it just comes out as a plain **Stew**. Nothing edible is wasted.

!!! tip "Cook big, cook rich"
    A stew's heartiness comes from *what* and *how much* you put in. Piling in more food, and richer food like cooked meat, makes a more filling stew worth far more stored food — so fill all eight slots rather than cooking a thin, one-item pot. Cooking always pays off over eating the ingredients raw.

!!! success "Happy bellies"
    Citizens who eat a warm stew come away content — *"I ate a warm stew."* A simmering pot near your homes is quiet, steady happiness as well as food.

!!! danger "Don't cook poison"
    If any ingredient you add is **poisoned**, the whole stew turns poisonous. Eating it inflicts Poison, and your citizens won't touch it — so it feeds no one. Check foraged plants and mushrooms before you toss them in the pot. See [Herbalism](herbalism.md) for what's safe.

### Let a Cook tend the pots

Learning **Stews** also unlocks the **Cook**, a citizen [crafter](../civilization/workshops.md) who keeps a **Kitchen's** stew pots simmering for you. Set up a Kitchen [workshop](../civilization/workshops.md), staff it with a Cook, and your settlement brews and refreshes its own stews — turning raw supplies into stored food without you standing over the fire.

## Salt and preservation

The **Food Preservation** research opens the two great tricks for holding food against harder days: **salting** and **drying**. It also lets your settlement keep a little more food in store than it strictly needs.

> *Salt, smoke, and a cool cellar hold the harvest against leaner days. The settlement may keep a little more than it needs, instead of letting the surplus spoil.*

Food Preservation is an Ancient-era study on the [research tree](../civilization/research-and-eras.md), and it's the gateway to the [Stews](#the-stone-cooking-pot) research above.

### Salting food

**Salt** rubs into a fresh or bland food and slows its rot, so it keeps about **twice as long** — roughly **+100%** to its shelf life.

To salt:

- [ ] Hold **Salt** in one hand and the perishable food in the **other** hand.
- [ ] **Hold use** (right-click) for a moment while it rubs in — you'll hear the gritty grind.
- [ ] The food's tooltip now reads **Salted**, and one salt is used up.

!!! note "What salt can and can't do"
    - Salting is a **one-time** treatment — a single pinch of salt seasons a *whole stack* at once, and you can't salt the same food twice.
    - Salt **preserves what's left**; it does not turn a Bland food back to Fresh.
    - You can't salt food that's already rotted into Spoiled Food — there's nothing left to save.

### Drying racks

A **Drying Rack** turns raw meat and fish into dried rations that **never spoil at all** — the ultimate way to bank a big catch or a good hunt. You build racks by carving a log with a bone blade; see [Woodworking](woodworking.md) for how to make and place them.

Using a rack is simple:

- [ ] Right-click the rack with a dryable food to **hang** it on a free spot. Each rack holds **four** spots.
- [ ] Wait — each piece dries on its own over a couple of minutes, with a wisp of smoke rising as it cures.
- [ ] Right-click **empty-handed** to take the finished dried food back. (Pull it early and you just get the raw item back.)

| Raw food | Dries into |
| --- | --- |
| Beef | Dried Beef |
| Porkchop | Dried Porkchop |
| Mutton | Dried Mutton |
| Chicken | Dried Chicken |
| Rabbit | Dried Rabbit |
| Cod | Dried Cod |
| Salmon | Dried Salmon |

!!! tip "Preservation is a trade-off"
    Dried food sits a little *below* freshly cooked meat in food value — but it lasts **forever**. Preservation trades a bit of peak nourishment for the certainty that your rations will still be good next season. For a hoard you won't touch for a while, dry it; for tonight's meal, cook it.

### Choosing a method

=== "Cook it (Stew)"

    **Best for a big, shared food store.** A stew is rot-proof, feeds many, and makes citizens happy — but it lives in the pot and drains as it's eaten. Great for anti-starvation.

=== "Salt it"

    **Best for a quick save.** Cheap and fast, and it roughly **doubles** any perishable's shelf life. Ideal for food you'll eat soon but not *right* now.

=== "Dry it"

    **Best for the long haul.** Dried meat and fish never spoil, so it's the way to bank a surplus indefinitely — at the cost of a little food value and a couple of minutes at the rack.

## Feeding the settlement

Your citizens don't eat straight from your pack — they draw on food stored in the settlement's claimed storage, and the [Town Hall](../civilization/settlements.md) tallies it all into a **food rate**.

Only **good** food counts. Spoiled and poisoned food contributes **no food rate**, even sitting in claimed storage, and the Town Hall will refuse a deposit that isn't *safe for the settlement*. That's exactly why the work on this page matters:

- **Stews** pour a large, rot-proof lump of food value into your stores.
- **Salted** food survives longer in storage before it turns.
- **Dried** rations stay valid indefinitely.

!!! tip "Read the food line"
    Hover the **Food** line on your Town Hall panel to see how much food per second your stored supplies are contributing. If the number is limp despite a full larder, you're probably storing food that's gone Bland or Spoiled — preserve the fresh catch before it rots. The [Starvation](../reference/codex-and-ponder.md) chronicle entry walks through building a stable stored-food rate.
