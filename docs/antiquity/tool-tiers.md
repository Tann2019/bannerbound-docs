# Tool Tiers & Ages

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Every civilization is measured by its edge. You begin with splinters of bone and flakes of flint, climb through knapped stone, and finally draw copper, tin, and bronze from the rock. This page is the whole ladder in one place — what each tier is, where it comes from, and the single rule that decides which tools your people are allowed to pick up.</em></p>

!!! abstract "The short version"
    - Tools climb through tiers: <span class="bb-tier bone">Bone</span> → <span class="bb-tier stone">Stone</span> → <span class="bb-tier copper">Copper</span> / <span class="bb-tier tin">Tin</span> → <span class="bb-tier bronze">Bronze</span> (with a Wood tier tucked between bone and stone, and Iron waiting beyond).
    - Your **settlement** has a current **tool age**. Workers can only equip tools **up to** that age — hand a stone-age tribe a bronze pick and you'll be told *"That tool is too advanced for your current tool age."*
    - **Research advances the age.** Each step is a deliberate unlock, not something you drift into.
    - **Most tools are a head plus a haft.** A knapped or cast **head / blade / point** is useless until you lash it to a **stick** — *"You need a stick to haft this."*
    - **Bone tools** are assembled from parts. **Stone tools** are knapped, then hafted. **Metal tools** are cast, then cold-hammered.
    - Special tools break the pattern: the **Stone Hammer**, **tongs**, the **Stone Chisel**, and the **Bone Saw** each do one specific job.

## The tool age — the rule behind everything

Your settlement always sits at one **tool age**. It's a civilization-wide setting, not a per-citizen one, and it acts as a ceiling: a worker may equip any tool **of the current age or below**, and nothing above it. Founding a settlement starts you in the **Bone Age**; every step up the ladder is opened by a specific piece of [research](../civilization/research-and-eras.md).

| Tool age | Opened by | Worker tools become | Weapon damage |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| <span class="bb-tier bone">Bone</span> **Bone Age** | Founding your settlement | Bone tools + a flint knife | 4.0 |
| **Wood Age** | *Woodworking* research | Wooden tools | 4.0 |
| <span class="bb-tier stone">Stone</span> **Stone Age** | *Knapping* research | Stone tools | 5.0 |
| <span class="bb-tier copper">Copper</span> **Copper Age** | *Metal Working* research | Copper tools | 5.5 |
| <span class="bb-tier bronze">Bronze</span> **Bronze Age** | *Bronze Age* research (advances your era to Classical) | Bronze tools | 6.0 |
| **Iron Age** | *Iron Working* research (advances your era to Medieval) | Iron tools | 6.0 |

!!! note "Where tin fits"
    There is no separate *Tin Age*. Copper and tin are the two **pure metals** of the same rank — tin tools are cast exactly like copper, but tin is a soft, second-rate metal whose real calling is to marry copper into **bronze**. When your workers reach the Copper Age, think of it as the whole copper-and-tin metal tier opening at once. Bronze is the alloy that sits a full rank above both.

Iron is the capstone that carries you out of Antiquity and into the Medieval era — it's the far end of this ladder, covered alongside the rest of the metal chain in [Metalworking](metalworking.md).

### How a worker actually gets a tool

Assigning the age is only half of it — the citizen still needs the physical tool in hand. Open a worker's job tab and you'll see the **tool slot** it needs filled. You can drop a valid tool straight in, or mark a **tool storage** container so workers of that type pull the right tool automatically. If the slot demands more than your age allows, you'll get the *too advanced* message; give them the age-appropriate tool instead. See [Jobs & Labor](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md) for staffing and tool storage.

## Anatomy of a tool — head, haft, and hand

Past the very first bone crafts, nearly every tool in Antiquity is **two pieces**: a working **head, blade, or point** that does the cutting or digging, and a wooden **haft** — a plain stick — that you grip. Try to finish a head without one and the game stops you cold:

> You need a stick to haft this.

That stick (usually two, plus a wrap of **plant fiber** to bind it) is the difference between a loose lump of shaped metal and a real tool. The head is where the tier lives; the haft is almost always the same humble stick no matter how grand the metal.

There are **three ways** a head comes to be, and they're the real story of this page:

- **Assembled** — Bone tools skip the two-step entirely: you pile bone blades, sticks, and fiber on a [Crafting Stone](knapping.md) and they come out whole.
- **Knapped** — Stone heads are *shaped by hand* in the [knapping](knapping.md) minigame, then hafted at the Crafting Stone. A well-worked head even **carries its quality** through to the finished tool.
- **Cast, then forged** — Metal heads are poured molten into a fired mould set on a [Stone Anvil](metalworking.md), then **cold-hammered** on that same anvil with a stick to become a finished, quality-graded tool.

## The tiers, bone to bronze

### <span class="bb-tier bone">Bone</span> Bone Age

Your scavenger's toolkit. Assemble **bone** blades and sticks on a **Crafting Stone** — no furnace, no bench, just piled parts and a sneak-click. This tier is broad but crude, and it's the widest tool set you'll ever make purely by hand.

| Bone tool | What it's for | Made from |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Bone Pickaxe** | Mining soft stone and ore | 3 Bone Blades + 2 sticks + plant fiber |
| **Bone Axe** | Chopping wood | Bone Blades + sticks + fiber |
| **Bone Shovel** | Digging dirt, sand, gravel | Bone Blades + sticks + fiber |
| **Bone Hoe** | Tilling for farms | Bone Blades + sticks + fiber |
| **Bone Sword** | Your first real weapon | Bone Blades + sticks + fiber |
| **Bone Knife** | Cutting plants, carving benches (durable) | Bone Blade + stick + fiber |
| **Bone Club** | A blunt starter weapon | Bone parts |
| **Bone Shears** | Shearing wool and leaves | Bone parts |
| **Bone Spear** | Thrown or thrust — hunting and defence | Bone parts |

Alongside these live the true first edges — the disposable **Flint Blade** and **Bone Blade** you knap straight off a stone surface, and the durable **Flint Knife** you assemble in your inventory grid. The [knapping](knapping.md) page covers those in full; here it's enough to know they're the seed the whole tier grows from.

!!! tip "Bone is a spring, not a home"
    Bone tools exist to get you off the ground and to the point where you can research your way upward. They're slow and they wear fast. The moment **Knapping** is within reach, take it.

### <span class="bb-tier stone">Stone</span> Stone tools

Researching **Knapping** advances you to the Stone Age and unlocks the full stone tool set. Stone is the first tier that follows the head-and-haft pattern properly: you **knap a head** in a timing minigame — mistime it badly and *"You broke the stone"* — then pile the head with sticks and fiber on the Crafting Stone to haft it into the finished tool.

| Stone tool | Knapped head | Hafted with |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Stone Pickaxe** | Stone Pickaxe Head | 2 sticks + fiber |
| **Stone Axe** | Stone Axe Head | sticks + fiber |
| **Stone Shovel** | Stone Shovel Head | sticks + fiber |
| **Stone Hoe** | Stone Hoe Head | sticks + fiber |
| **Stone Sword** | Stone Sword Blade | sticks + fiber |
| **Stone Spear** | Stone Spear Point | 2 sticks + 2 fiber |
| **Stone Chisel** | *(knapped whole from two rocks)* | — |
| **Stone Hammer** | Stone Hammer Head | stick + fiber |

Because a cleanly knapped head **transfers its quality** to the tool, patient knapping pays off twice — once in the head, again in the finished pick or blade. Stone is slower and weaker than the metal to come, but it's the bridge from a scavenging camp to a settlement that can quarry, build, and arm itself in earnest. The full walkthrough lives on the [Knapping & First Tools](knapping.md) page.

### <span class="bb-tier copper">Copper</span> / <span class="bb-tier tin">Tin</span> The pure metals

Here the whole process changes. Metal tools are **cast**, not carved. Drop raw ore into a **crucible** — each piece adds **50 mB** of molten metal — and heat it inside a **Bloomery** until the whole charge turns liquid. Carry the glowing crucible to a **Stone Anvil**, set a **fired clay mould** on the anvil, and pour to cast a rough **head, blade, or point**. Then finish it right there: stack the cast part on the anvil with a stick and **cold-hammer** it into a graded tool.

That hammer minigame grades the tool from **Crude** through **Standard** and **Fine** to **Masterwork**, and quality drives durability, effectiveness, and a shot at a special perk.

Each metal tier casts the same family of parts. The mould decides both the shape and how much metal a pour draws:

| Cast part | Becomes | Metal per pour | Finishing step |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Axe / Pickaxe **head** | Axe / Pickaxe | 150 mB | Cold-hammer + stick haft |
| Hoe / Shovel **head** | Hoe / Shovel | 100 mB | Cold-hammer + stick haft |
| **Blade** | Sword | 200 mB | Cold-hammer + stick haft |
| **Knife head** | Knife | 80 mB | Cold-hammer + stick haft |
| **Hammer head** | Hammer | 180 mB | Cold-hammer + stick haft |
| **Spear point** | Spear | 120 mB | Hafted at the Crafting Stone (2 sticks + 2 fiber) — no anvil needed |
| **Arrowhead** | Arrow | 60 mB | Fletched at the [Fletching Station](fletching-and-archery.md) |
| **Chisel** | Chisel | 60 mB | Cast to shape — no haft |
| **Ingot** | Tin / Bronze ingot | 100 mB | Cast as stock metal |

A pour only fills up to what the mould needs, and each raw ore piece adds **50 mB** — so an axe head (150 mB) wants three pieces, a sword blade (200 mB) four, with any leftover metal staying in the crucible to cool or top up. The full melt, moulds, and bloomery are laid out on the [Metalworking](metalworking.md) page; treat this table as *what exists*, and that page as *how to pour it*.

!!! note "Copper leads, tin trails"
    **Copper** is your first genuine metal tier — a clear step up from stone, and the metal your workers actually equip at the Copper Age. **Tin** casts into the identical tool set, but it's soft and short-lived; its true value is in **tongs** and in being melted together with copper to make bronze. Don't expect a tin pickaxe to carry a settlement.

!!! tip "Bank your quality, and anneal to save it"
    Each clean strike work-hardens the metal and narrows the band. **Sneak-click** to stop and bank the tier you've reached — safe. But a miss on hardened metal **cracks** it and drops a tier. If a piece is getting brittle, carry it back to a **Bloomery** and hold the anneal band: annealing resets it to wide-and-slow without losing the hardness you earned, so careful smiths keep climbing.

### <span class="bb-tier bronze">Bronze</span> The alloy

Bronze is no ore — you **alloy** it. Charge one crucible with **both copper and tin** and melt them together; there is no bronze vein anywhere in the world. The proportions decide everything: keep **copper at 60–90%** and **tin at 10–40%** of the charge and the whole melt resolves to clean **Molten Bronze**. Stray outside that band — too little tin, or a third metal in the pot — and it slumps to a poorer, off-ratio result. The Bloomery readout shows the running composition and a ✓ / ✗ ratio check as you charge.

Bronze pours into the **same moulds** as copper and tin but casts a **harder, longer-lasting** tool — the backbone of the age and the proving ground before iron. Researching the Bronze Age also advances your civilization's era to Classical, so this tier is a genuine milestone, not just a better pick.

!!! warning "A stone hammer can't finish bronze"
    A hammer carries a **rank** of its own, and a workpiece only reaches top quality when the hammer is close enough in rank to the metal. A **Stone Hammer** (or a copper/tin one) forges copper and tin to full **Masterwork** — but bronze sits a rank higher, and a stone hammer will **cap a bronze tool at Standard** no matter how clean your strikes. To forge top-quality bronze, hammer it with a **copper, tin, or bronze** hammer. Cast yourself a proper metal hammer *before* you start swinging on bronze.

## Tool types at a glance

The tier changes, but the *jobs* the tools do stay constant across the whole ladder. This is every tool type and how its working end is made:

| Tool | What it does | How the head is made | Needs a haft? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Axe** | Fells trees, shapes wood | Knapped (stone) or cast (metal) | Yes — stick |
| **Pickaxe** | Mines stone and ore | Knapped or cast | Yes — stick |
| **Shovel** | Digs dirt, sand, gravel | Knapped or cast | Yes — stick |
| **Hoe** | Tills farmland | Knapped or cast | Yes — stick |
| **Sword** | Melee weapon | Knapped blade or cast blade | Yes — stick |
| **Knife** | Cuts plants, carves benches | Assembled (bone) or cast head | Yes — stick |
| **Hammer** | Dresses anvils, cold-hammers metal | Knapped or cast head | Yes — stick |
| **Chisel** | Masonry; dressing worked stone | Knapped (stone) or cast whole (metal) | No |
| **Spear** | Thrown or thrust; hunting, fishing, defence | Knapped point or cast point | Yes — hafted at the Crafting Stone |
| **Arrows** | Ammunition for bows | Cast arrowhead | Fletched at the Fletching Station |

## Special tools

A handful of tools aren't about mining and fighting — they're the keys that unlock whole crafts. Keep these on hand:

=== ":material-hammer: Stone Hammer"

    The smith's first tool, knapped from a **Stone Hammer Head** and a haft. It does two irreplaceable jobs: **right-click a Stone block** with it to *dress* the block into a **Stone Anvil** in place, and use it to **cold-hammer** cast parts into finished tools on that anvil. It forges copper and tin to full quality — but remember, it caps **bronze** at Standard. See [Metalworking](metalworking.md).

=== ":material-tongs: Tongs"

    A freshly-melted crucible is a glowing, roughly 1000 °C clay pot — carry it bare-handed and it sets you alight and burns you each second. Hold **tongs in your off-hand** and the heat pours into the tongs' durability instead of your health. They come in four grades: **Wooden Tongs** char out fast (the desperate pre-metal stopgap that lets you lift your very first melt), while cast **Tin**, **Copper**, and **Bronze Tongs** last far longer. Tongs are not a weapon or a digging tool — they simply let you move molten metal safely.

=== ":material-chisel: Stone Chisel"

    The **mason's** tool, knapped from two rocks after researching Masonry. Set two stone blocks side by side and right-click one with the Stone Chisel to lash up a **Mason's Bench**, then use it to run the chisel-strike step that dresses rough stone into slabs, stairs, walls, bricks, and polished blocks in batches. See [Masonry](masonry.md).

=== ":material-saw-blade: Bone Saw"

    The **woodworker's** tool, made from a couple of bone blades and sticks on the Crafting Stone. Place two logs side by side and right-click them with it to form a **Woodworking Table**, then use it to run the sawing minigame that turns logs into sticks, handles, and worked wood. See [Woodworking](woodworking.md).

## The gate in play

The tool age isn't just flavour — it shapes your day-to-day play in two ways you'll actually run into:

!!! warning "Too advanced for the age"
    Drop a tool above your settlement's tier into a worker's slot and it simply won't take: *"That tool is too advanced for your current tool age."* The fix is always research, not a workaround — advance the age, then the tool becomes usable. This stops you from short-cutting the tech tree by handing out looted or gifted gear.

!!! warning "Too soft to chip this ore"
    A pickaxe also has to be **hard enough** for what it's digging. Send a miner against a stubborn ore with too weak a pick and you'll see *"…'s pickaxe is too soft to chip this ore — give them a better one."* Tougher veins want a higher-tier pickaxe, so keep your miners equipped a rung up as your quarries go deeper.

## How the tiers compare

Higher tiers don't just look better — they **work faster and hit harder**. As you climb the ladder, the same worker chops, mines, and harvests noticeably quicker, and weapons bite deeper:

| Tier | Chopping | Mining | Harvesting | Weapon damage |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| <span class="bb-tier bone">Bone</span> | Slowest | Slowest | Slowest | 4.0 |
| **Wood** | Very slow | Very slow | Very slow | 4.0 |
| <span class="bb-tier stone">Stone</span> | Moderate | Moderate | Moderate | 5.0 |
| <span class="bb-tier copper">Copper</span> | Fast | Fast | Fast | 5.5 |
| <span class="bb-tier bronze">Bronze</span> | Faster | Faster | Faster | 6.0 |
| **Iron** | Fastest | Fastest | Fastest | 6.0 |

Attack **speed** stays the same across every tier — it's the damage per swing that climbs. On top of the tier, **forge quality** (Crude → Standard → Fine → Masterwork) stacks its own bonus to durability and effectiveness, so a Masterwork copper tool can rival a rushed bronze one. It's always worth taking the extra care at the anvil.
