# Tannery & Leather

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Every strap, satchel, and helmet lining in your settlement starts as a bloody hide on a wooden frame. The tannery is the only place that hide becomes leather — learn its three steps and you'll never want for it.</em></p>

!!! abstract "TL;DR"
    - **Hides** drop from cattle, sheep, pigs, goats, and horses — from hunting them or culling your herds. Each carries a **Hide Quality** of Poor, Standard, or Great.
    - Build a **Tanning Rack** by right-clicking a **2×2 square of logs** with a blade or knife. It holds **one hide at a time**.
    - **Scrape** a raw hide (right-click with a cutting tool, then swipe **six times**) → **Scraped Hide**. Better quality yields more.
    - **Cure** it: stack a **Clay Tank** beside the rack, fill it with water, add **Quicklime** to make curing liquid, then dip the scraped hide → **Cured Hide**.
    - **Dry** the cured hide back on the rack, then right-click empty-handed to collect **Leather**.
    - Turn leather (and metal) into armor at the **Armorer's Workbench**.

The tannery is your settlement's *only* source of leather — there is no crafting-table shortcut. Anything that needs leather, from a waterskin to an armorer's helmet lining, traces back to a hide scraped, cured, and dried here.

---

## Hides: the raw material

A **hide** is what you strip from a slain grazing animal. Five species give them:

| Animal | Hide item |
| --- | --- |
| Cow | **Cow Hide** |
| Sheep | **Sheep Hide** |
| Pig | **Pig Hide** |
| Goat | **Goat Hide** |
| Horse | **Horse Hide** |

Hides come from two places:

- **The hunt.** Wild animals killed out in the world drop a hide of the appropriate species. See [Herbalism & the Hunt](herbalism.md) for gathering out in the wild.
- **The herd.** When your settlement culls a domesticated animal it has been raising, that animal yields a hide too — no weapon needed.

Every hide is stamped with a **Hide Quality**, and that grade follows it all the way to the finished leather.

| Quality | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| **Poor** | A rough, damaged hide. You always get *something*, but it scrapes down to little. |
| **Standard** | A serviceable hide — the everyday result. |
| **Great** | A clean, prime hide. Scrapes down to the most leather. |

!!! info "Quality carries through"
    Better hide quality doesn't change the *steps* — it changes the *yield*. A Great hide scraped by hand gives you more scraped hide (and so more leather) than a Poor one. Good hunting and good husbandry quite literally pay off at the rack.

### How Hide Quality is decided

Quality is set at the moment the animal dies, and the two acquisition paths judge it differently.

=== "Hunting (wild animals)"
    Wild game is graded on **the weapon you used** versus that species' **preferred weapon**. Each animal has a "right way" to be taken:

    | Animal | Prefers | Weapon category |
    | --- | --- | --- |
    | Cow | Spear | Spear |
    | Horse | Spear | Spear |
    | Pig | Blade | Blades & cutting tools |
    | Sheep | Blunt | Blunt weapons (the bone club) |
    | Goat | Arrow | Bows & arrows |

    - Kill it with its **preferred** weapon → **Great** hide.
    - Kill it with any other *valid* hunting weapon → **Standard** hide.
    - Kill it with something that isn't a hunting weapon at all (fists, a pickaxe) → **Poor** hide. You still get a hide — just a rough one.

    The four weapon categories are **Blade**, **Spear**, **Arrow**, and **Blunt**. Match the animal to the tool and clean kills become prime hides. (Chasing goats with a bow? See [Fletching & Archery](fletching-and-archery.md).)

=== "Herding (your own livestock)"
    A culled animal you raised is graded on **how well it lived** and **how skilled the herder is** — no weapon involved. Two things push the grade up:

    - **Living conditions** — a well-fed, well-kept herd produces better hides than a neglected one.
    - **Herder skill** — an experienced herder culls cleanly; a novice botches it.

    Keep your animals healthy and let your herders gain experience, and your culls will trend from Standard toward Great. A miserable, poorly-tended herd slides toward Poor.

!!! tip "Aim your hunts"
    If you're out hunting specifically for leather, carry the *right* tool for the game you expect. A **spear** for cattle and horses, a **blade** for pigs, the **bone club** for sheep, and a **bow** for goats will turn ordinary kills into Great hides — and Great hides mean more leather per animal.

## Building the Tannery

The tannery is two blocks working together: a **Tanning Rack** where hides are worked, and a **Clay Tank** beside it that holds the curing liquid.

### The Tanning Rack

You don't craft the rack in a grid — you *build it in place*. Lay down a **2×2 square of logs**, then **right-click** them with a **blade or knife**. The logs snap together into a **Tanning Rack**, and the cutting tool loses a little durability for the effort.

The rack holds **one hide at a time**. It's the stage for two of the three steps — scraping the raw hide, and later drying the cured one — so a busy tannery wants several racks side by side.

!!! warning "Any log will do — the tool won't"
    The rack forms from a 2×2 of logs, but it must be a **cutting tool** (a blade or knife) that you right-click with. Bare hands, a pickaxe, or an axe won't shape it.

### The Clay Tank

Stack a **Clay Tank** right beside the rack. The tank is a fired-clay vessel (shaped and kiln-fired over in [Pottery](pottery.md)) and it **stacks vertically** — **each piece you add gives another eight buckets of capacity**. One piece is enough to get started; stack more when you're curing hides in bulk.

To charge it:

1. **Fill it with water** — pour a bucket in.
2. **Add Quicklime** — right-click the water with **Quicklime** and it turns into **curing liquid**. (Quicklime is burnt limestone, produced by your masons — see [Masonry](masonry.md).)

A tank full of curing liquid is the beating heart of the tannery. Each hide you cure draws **one bucket** of curing out of it, so an eight-bucket tank cures eight hides before it needs charging again.

!!! danger "No curing liquid, no tannery"
    A tannery **won't run** without a clay tank of curing liquid inside it. Fill the tank with plain water and forget the quicklime and your hides will sit soaking forever — they only become cured hide once the water has been turned to curing liquid.

## From hide to leather

Three steps, in order: **scrape → cure → dry**. Here's the full walk-through by hand.

!!! example "Raw hide to leather, step by step"
    - [ ] **Drape** a raw hide over the tanning rack.
    - [ ] **Scrape** it: right-click with a cutting tool and hold left-click to **swipe** — **six swipes** clean it into a **Scraped Hide**.
    - [ ] **Charge** the clay tank: fill it with water, then add **Quicklime** to make curing liquid.
    - [ ] **Cure**: dip the Scraped Hide into the tank to draw a bucket of curing → **Cured Hide**.
    - [ ] **Dry**: hang the Cured Hide back on the rack and wait — it dries in about a minute.
    - [ ] **Collect**: once it's dry, right-click the rack **empty-handed** to take your **Leather**.

### 1 · Scrape the raw hide

Drape a raw hide over the rack, then **right-click it with a cutting tool** to begin scraping. Hold **left-click and swipe** across the hide — the on-screen prompt reads *"Hold left-click and swipe to scrape the hide."* After **six swipes** the flesh side comes clean and you're left with a **Scraped Hide**.

This is where quality cashes in: a **Great** hide scrapes down to more scraped hide than a **Poor** one, so prime hunts and healthy herds turn into noticeably more leather.

### 2 · Cure the scraped hide

Take your Scraped Hide to the charged clay tank and **dip it** into the curing liquid. It soaks up a bucket's worth and comes out a **Cured Hide** — the tank's supply drops by one bucket. Keep the quicklime coming and you can cure hide after hide until the tank runs dry.

### 3 · Dry into leather

Hang the **Cured Hide** back on the tanning rack. It dries in place (you'll see it settling for about a minute), and once dry it's finished. **Right-click the rack empty-handed** to lift off your **Leather**.

That leather goes straight into your economy — waterskins, bindings, satchels, and the armor below.

## Armor: the Armorer's Workbench

Leather isn't just a trade good — it's the soft half of your first real armor. Craft and design armor at the **Armorer's Workbench**, a two-block bench you place like a table.

**Shift + right-click** either half of the bench to open its design screen. Inside, a live 3D **helmet** hangs in the center — **drag to spin it** and inspect your work from every angle — and you dress it up **zone by zone**. A helmet has four zones:

Dome
: The crown of the head.

Front
: The brow and face guard.

Cheeks
: The side plates.

Neck
: The rear neck guard.

Each zone is its own toggle — **click it to cycle** through three settings, and set every zone independently:

| Setting | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| **Off** | Left bare — no plating on that zone. |
| **Leather** | Clad in your tannery's finished leather. |
| **Metal** | Plated in metal cast at the forge — see [Metalworking](metalworking.md). |

Mix and match: a leather cap with a **metal** dome and cheeks costs less metal than a full metal helm but still turns a blow. As your metalworking matures you can commit to a full **metal helmet** for the front line and keep leather for everyone else. The finished piece shows the material it was built from.

!!! tip "Leather first, metal later"
    In the Stone and early Bronze Age, leather is what you *have* — hides are renewable and the tannery is cheap to run. Lean on all-leather kit early, then upgrade key zones to metal once your bloomery and smithy are producing bars.

## Letting a crafter run the tannery

Scraping every hide by hand is satisfying, but your settlement can do it for you. Designate a room around your rack and tank as a **Tannery** [workshop](../civilization/workshops.md) and assign a **Crafter** to it. The crafter becomes your tanner and walks the *entire* leather chain on their own.

The one hard requirement: the workshop **must contain a clay tank**, and that tank must hold curing liquid for the tanner to work. Without one, the workshop reports it's **missing curing liquid** and stalls.

Once staffed, your tanner will, all on their own:

- **Scoop water** from a nearby source into the tank and **work in quicklime** to make curing liquid.
- **Scrape** stocked raw hides at the rack.
- **Cure** the scraped hides in the tank.
- **Lay cured hides out to dry**, then **collect the finished leather** once it's ready.

Keep the tannery **stocked** and the tanner keeps producing. It wants a steady supply of **raw hides** and **Quicklime** on hand, plus a **fired clay bucket** for hauling water — your [stocker](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md) will fetch these as leather is ordered or your minimum stock runs low.

!!! tip "Hand-scrape your best hides"
    The NPC tanner scrapes every hide at the same fixed, standard yield — it doesn't track quality. So when you land a **Great** hide from a well-aimed hunt, consider scraping *that one* yourself: the minigame rewards the extra quality with extra scraped hide, squeezing more leather out of your finest kills than the automated line ever would.
