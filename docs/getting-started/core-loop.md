# How to Play (The Core Loop)

<p class="bb-lead">You founded a settlement. The campfire is lit, a citizen or two are milling about, and the world is quiet. Now what? This is the rhythm you will settle into for the rest of the game — the loop that turns one campfire into a civilization.</p>

!!! abstract "The loop in one breath"
    **Gather** food and materials with citizen jobs → **research** new crafts, jobs, and eras with the Science and Culture you build up → **grow** by feeding a surplus that attracts immigrants → **build & defend** with workshops, walls, and guards → **advance** to the next era and widen your borders. Then do it all again, larger. Almost everything you order flows through the **town hall menus** and the **Foreman's Rod**.

## You lead — you do not solo

Bannerbound is a civilization game, not a survival grind. You are the **leader** of a band of citizens, not a lone adventurer. Your hands still swing a pickaxe when you need them to, but the point of the game is not what *you* mine — it is what your *people* do. You organize their work, unlock what they know how to make, and grow the settlement around them. The citizens gather, haul, craft, and build; you decide *what* and *where*.

That mental shift is the whole game. When you want more wood, you do not go chop a forest — you assign a **Forester** and mark where to fell. When you want stronger tools, you do not grind for them — you research the knowledge and let a crafter make them. Your job is direction, not labor.

!!! tip "Think in surpluses, not stacks"
    A lone survival player counts the logs in their inventory. A leader counts the *rate*: is food coming in faster than it is eaten? Is wood arriving faster than the builders use it? Nearly every decision in the loop is about widening a surplus so the settlement can grow into it.

## Your two control surfaces

You steer everything through exactly two tools. Learn these and the rest of the game opens up.

### The town hall (your campfire)

Right-click your **Campfire** to open the **Town Hall**. This is the command center of your settlement — every high-level decision lives here, sorted into menus and tabs:

| Menu | What you do there | Full page |
| --- | --- | --- |
| :material-flask: **Research** | Spend Science and Culture to unlock crafts, jobs, storage, policies, and whole new eras | [Research & Eras](../civilization/research-and-eras.md) |
| :material-account-group: **Citizens** | Review each person — their job, mood, health, relationships, and thoughts | [Citizens](../civilization/citizens.md) |
| :material-hammer-wrench: **Labor** | See who works what, and balance your workforce across jobs | [Jobs & Labor](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md) |
| :material-map: **Territory** | Claim more chunks to expand your borders | [Territory](../civilization/territory.md) |
| :material-scale-balance: **Policies** | Enact settlement-wide rules that shape how your people live and work | [Government](../civilization/government.md) |
| :material-vote: **Votes** | Call and settle the decisions your government puts to the people | [Government](../civilization/government.md) |
| :material-handshake: **Diplomacy** | Deal with neighboring settlements — trade, treaties, or hostility | [Diplomacy](../civilization/diplomacy.md) |
| :material-hands-pray: **Faith** | Found or adopt a faith once your people are ready | [Faith](../civilization/faith.md) |

You will also find **Walls** and **Statistics** here as your settlement matures.

### The Foreman's Rod (your pointer)

The town hall menus decide *what*. The **Foreman's Rod** decides *where*. It is the tool you hold to mark worker areas out in the world — **mines, fields, pens, quarries, guard posts, and other managed work sites**. Point it, click two corners or a block, and your citizens go to work on exactly that spot.

Want a farm? Assign a Farmer in the Labor menu, then take the Rod out and mark the field. Want that ore vein worked? Mark the deposit with the Rod and a Miner claims it. The pattern is always the same: **assign the job at the town hall, then place the work with the Rod.**

!!! warning "A job with no marked site sits idle"
    Assigning a citizen to a gatherer job is only half the order. If a Farmer has no field marked, a Herder has no pen, or a Digger has no area, they will stand around with nothing to do. If a worker looks idle, the fix is usually a click of the Foreman's Rod — or a missing tool or drop-off chest.

## The five pillars of the loop

Everything you do rolls through these five stages, over and over, at a bigger scale each lap.

### 1. Gather

People cannot eat plans. Your first and forever priority is bringing in **food** and raw **materials**, and that means putting citizens on **gatherer jobs**. Open the **Labor** menu (or click a citizen), pick a job, then mark their work area with the Foreman's Rod.

The food-getters:

- **Forager** — gathers wild plants and food from the land.
- **Farmer** — tills, plants, and harvests marked fields, stocking the granary.
- **Fisher** — fishes nearby water and drops the catch at storage.
- **Hunter** — hunts wild animals on unclaimed land; you pick the prey.
- **Herder** — raises and breeds penned animals, culling the surplus for meat.

The material-getters:

- **Forester** — fells trees and hauls the logs home.
- **Miner** — mines ore from claimed underground veins.
- **Digger** — clears and mines stone and blocks inside areas you mark; with research, Diggers are promoted into **Quarryworkers**, who can mine stone and coal ore too.

Gathered goods flow to **drop-off** storage, where crafters and builders draw on them — and citizens on hauling duty (**Stockers**) carry goods between drop-offs and workshops, so nothing waits on you to lug it around. Keep at least one steady food source running from the very start — a settlement that stops eating stops everything else too.

[:material-arrow-right: Jobs & Labor](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md) · [Food](../civilization/food.md)

### 2. Research

As your settlement lives and works, it slowly generates two currencies of progress: **Science** and **Culture**. You spend them in the **Research** menu to unlock new crafts, new jobs, better storage, new policies — and eventually to advance your entire **era**.

Research is the spine of the whole game. Almost everything you *cannot* do yet, you cannot do because you have not researched it. A worker who refuses a deposit because "your people don't know how to work this yet," a tool tier just out of reach, a policy you wish you could enact — the answer is nearly always the research tree. The tree has a **Science** side and a **Culture** side, so a well-rounded settlement invests in both.

!!! tip "Research is passive — set it and forget it"
    You do not grind Science by hand. It accumulates while your people live and work, and completes whatever you have queued. Keep something useful queued at all times so no progress is wasted.

[:material-arrow-right: Research & Eras](../civilization/research-and-eras.md)

### 3. Grow

**People are the engine.** More citizens means more hands for every job — but every new citizen is also another mouth. A settlement grows by drawing in **immigrants**, and it only draws them in when it can support them.

Two levers matter most:

- **Food surplus** — a well-fed settlement with food to spare attracts newcomers; a starving one repels them. Grow your food *ahead* of your population, not behind it.
- **Housing** — new arrivals need beds. Build homes before you need them, not after people are already sleeping rough.

As your numbers climb, your settlement itself advances through stages — from a humble **Hearth** to a **Tribe**, then a **Village**, a **Town**, and finally a **City** — each a milestone that unlocks more of what your people can become. You can also invite **other players** to join as members using **Registration Tablets** issued from the town hall.

!!! warning "Grow food and beds before population, always"
    The most common early stumble is a growth spurt with nothing to catch it — new citizens arrive, find no home and no spare food, and their resentment festers. Keep a comfortable margin of both ahead of your headcount.

[:material-arrow-right: Housing](../civilization/housing.md) · [Settlements](../civilization/settlements.md)

### 4. Build & defend

With materials flowing and knowledge unlocked, you turn raw gathering into finished things — and you protect what you have made.

- **Workshops** are areas you mark (with the Rod) around a work block; a **Crafter** bound there fulfills your orders and gains skill. This is how logs become planks, stone becomes tools, and so on.
- **Walls** enclose and protect your settlement. Design and construct them from the town hall's Walls tab.
- **Guards** patrol your borders and fight off raiders and hostile mobs — steadier in a brawl than an ordinary citizen, and they won't wander off into the wild. You post them with the Foreman's Rod.

The wilds are not always kind. **Barbarians** and other threats will test a settlement that leaves itself open, so fold defense into the loop rather than bolting it on after the first raid.

[:material-arrow-right: Workshops](../civilization/workshops.md) · [Walls](../civilization/walls.md) · [Barbarians](../civilization/barbarians.md)

### 5. Advance

Each full lap of the loop should leave you stronger than the last. The endgame of the loop is **momentum**: push your **era** forward through research, **claim more territory** to reach new resources, and **meet your neighbors** — as trading partners or rivals. New eras raise your ceilings across the board, from the tools your workers wield to the number of players you can register.

Then the loop begins again, but everything is bigger: more citizens gathering more, more Science funding deeper research, more land to defend, more ambition within reach.

## The rhythm of a day

The five pillars play out against Minecraft's day/night cycle, and each part of the day has its own character.

=== ":material-white-balance-sunny: Day — work"

    Daylight is the working shift. Assigned citizens are out gathering, hauling, and crafting; builders raise structures; guards patrol. This is when your orders actually get done. Spend the day directing: check who is idle, mark new work sites with the Rod, and queue up research.

=== ":material-weather-sunset: Dusk — socialize (and unrest surfaces)"

    As the sun sets, citizens knock off work and **socialize** — mingling, building relationships, and feeding the settlement's sense of community. But dusk is also when trouble comes to a head. If people are unhappy, the warning arrives now: *"As night falls, unrest stirs in the settlement."*

=== ":material-weather-night: Night — sleep"

    After socializing, citizens head to their beds and **sleep**, recovering the stamina they will spend tomorrow. A citizen with no home has nowhere to rest — and that shows up as deepening resentment. This is the quiet part of the loop, and a good moment to plan your next day.

### Watch the dusk warnings

Dusk is your daily check-in on morale. When something is wrong, the settlement tells you plainly:

- **Homeless citizens** — anyone without a home grows more resentful each day they go without a bed. Build housing.
- **Strike risk** — workers unhappy enough will simply **refuse to work tomorrow**. Idle, sulking citizens produce nothing.
- **Coup risk** — let discontent fester and, at dawn, *"the people may rise against the Chief."* Push a leader too far and the citizens will depose them.

!!! danger "Unhappiness compounds overnight"
    Resentment is not a one-day problem. Homeless and hungry citizens get *angrier* every night, and a strike or coup can undo a run of good progress in a single dawn. When the dusk warning appears, treat it as the day's most important task — fix the cause (food, beds, comfort) before you chase anything else.

## Two places to look things up

Bannerbound gives you two information surfaces, and they do different jobs. Knowing which to check saves a lot of guesswork.

### The Chronicle — press ++j++

The **Chronicle** is your settlement's permanent book of knowledge. Press ++j++ to open it. It **fills as you play** — discovering systems, researching ideas, using new blocks, and surviving crises all add entries that stay forever. When you want to *understand* how something works, this is where you read. It even includes an interactive **Ponder** view for many mechanics, so you can watch a system explained rather than just read about it.

The Chronicle is discovery, not homework — you are never expected to read it cover to cover. It is a reference you grow into, always there when a question comes up.

### The Journal tracker — your live to-do list

The **Journal tracker** is the small on-screen list of *current* tasks. Unlike the Chronicle, it only shows up when something actually needs your attention, and it clears as you handle things. Think of it as the settlement tapping you on the shoulder: *this* is what to do next, right now. You can toggle it on or off with its keybind.

!!! info "Which is which?"
    **Chronicle (++j++)** = permanent knowledge, always available, for understanding. **Journal tracker** = live tasks, appears only when relevant, for doing. When you are stuck on *how*, open the Chronicle. When you are wondering *what next*, glance at the tracker.

## A day in the loop

!!! example "One lap, start to finish"
    - [x] **Morning:** Open the town hall. You have two unemployed citizens. Assign one as a **Forager** for food and one as a **Forester** for wood. Take out the Foreman's Rod and mark a patch of forest for the Forester to fell.
    - [x] **Midday:** Logs are piling up at the drop-off. You queue a **Science** research in the Research menu so your idle progress isn't wasted, and mark a **workshop** around a work block so a crafter can turn logs into planks.
    - [x] **Afternoon:** Food is comfortably in surplus. You build a couple of extra beds, anticipating an immigrant — grow the housing *before* the person arrives.
    - [x] **Dusk:** Citizens gather to socialize. No unrest warning tonight — everyone is fed and housed. Good.
    - [x] **Night:** Everyone sleeps and recovers stamina. You glance at the Journal tracker, see it is clear, and plan tomorrow: maybe a Fisher for a second food source, and a Guard once the walls go up.

    That is the loop. Tomorrow it repeats — a little larger every time.
