# Founding a Settlement

<p class="bb-lead">Every civilization starts as a spark. A single campfire in unclaimed land, one right-click, and a name — that is the moment a wandering settler becomes a founder.</p>

!!! abstract "The short version"
    1. Craft a **Campfire** and place it on **unclaimed land**. It starts **unlit**.
    2. **Right-click** the campfire to open **Found a Settlement**. Read the **Site Assessment**.
    3. Set a **name**, pick a **banner color** (must be unique), and choose a **Culture Style**.
    4. Confirm. The campfire **lights** — it is now your **town hall**.
    5. Make sure your **faction banner** is standing within **24 blocks** of the town hall with a clear side. If it ever falls, your settlement goes silent until you raise it again.

## Before you settle: the campfire

A settlement is not founded from a menu or a magic wand — it grows around a **Campfire**. If you have just arrived, gather a little wood and stone by hand, carve a [Crafting Stone](../antiquity/knapping.md) from cobblestone, and use it to make a campfire. The [First Hour](first-hour.md) walks through those first crafts step by step.

Now find your ground and **place the campfire on unclaimed land**. Here is the key detail most new founders miss:

!!! note "An unlit campfire is an invitation"
    In unclaimed land, a **newly placed campfire starts unlit**. That is normal — it is waiting for you. The flame only rises once you found a settlement on it. A lit campfire you find in the world already belongs to someone.

Choose the spot with a little care. This campfire becomes the permanent heart of your settlement — the town hall — so plant it somewhere you are happy to build around: near water, on grassy ground, with room to grow.

## Right-click to found

**Right-click the unlit campfire.** This opens the **Found a Settlement** screen, and the first thing it shows you is a **Site Assessment** — a quick read of the land around you before you commit.

If the ground is good, you will simply see *"This looks like a good place to settle."* Otherwise, the assessment flags what will hold you back:

| Warning | What it means | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| :material-water-off: **No water nearby** | There is no water in reach of the site | Fishing and livestock will suffer — two of your easiest early [food](../civilization/food.md) sources |
| :material-sprout-outline: **Barren ground** | Little grass around the site | Foraging and farming have little to work with |

!!! warning "You can settle anyway — but read the room"
    The Site Assessment is advice, not a wall. You *can* found on a barren, dry patch. But water and grass feed your citizens in the early game, and a bad site means a slow, hungry start. If the warnings look serious, it usually costs you nothing to walk a little farther and place the campfire somewhere kinder.

## Name, color, and Culture Style

Once you decide to proceed, you make three choices that define your settlement's identity.

=== "Settlement Name"

    Your settlement's name must be **unique on the server** — no two settlements can share one. Names use **3–24 characters**: letters, numbers, spaces, hyphens, underscores, and apostrophes. Pick something you will be proud to see on the map and in the chronicles of war and trade.

=== "Banner Color"

    Choose a **banner color** — the shade that marks your people on the map, on your borders, and across every menu. Like the name, your color must be **unique**; if another settlement already flies it, you will be asked to pick another. This is the seed of your [heraldry](../civilization/culture-and-heraldry.md), which you can refine into a full crest later.

=== "Culture Style"

    Finally, **choose a Culture Style** — the cultural character your settlement is founded with. It shapes the flavor of your people and their [culture](../civilization/culture-and-heraldry.md) as your civilization develops. Available styles depend on what your world offers at founding.

!!! tip "Names and colors are claimed first-come"
    On a busy server, the obvious names and the boldest colors go early. If you have a banner in mind, found sooner rather than later — you cannot take a color another settlement already owns.

When you confirm, the campfire **bursts into flame**. You have founded a settlement, and its heart is now lit.

## Your town hall: the lit campfire

That lit campfire is your **town hall** — the single most important block you own. Right-click it any time to open the settlement menus. Almost everything you do as a leader flows through here:

- :material-flask: **[Research](../civilization/research-and-eras.md)** — unlock new jobs, storage, walls, and whole eras of progress.
- :material-account-group: **[Citizens](../civilization/citizens.md)** — see your people, their health, stamina, and moods.
- :material-map: **[Territory](../civilization/territory.md)** — claim and expand the chunks your settlement controls.
- :material-hammer-wrench: **[Labor](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md)** — set which jobs your citizens prioritize and how many work each.
- :material-gavel: **Policies** — toggle the rules your settlement lives by.
- :material-vote: **Votes** — the decisions your [government](../civilization/government.md) puts to the people.

!!! note "At first you rule by influence, not decree"
    A freshly founded settlement has no government yet — you begin in **anarchy**. Your citizens aren't idle: they **self-organize into gatherers** on their own, foraging, fishing, and cutting wood to keep the young settlement fed. But you can't *pin* a particular citizen to a particular job until you **enact a code of laws** and choose a [government](../civilization/government.md) — a Chiefdom or a Council. Assign work too early and the town hall says so plainly: *"No code of laws — citizens won't obey assignments yet."*

As your settlement grows and eras turn, the town hall gains still more tabs — [faith](../civilization/faith.md), diplomacy, [walls](../civilization/walls.md), statistics, and later the [**crisis decisions**](../civilization/crises.md) that will test your leadership. For now, know that this campfire is where you rule from. Guard it.

!!! danger "If the town hall is destroyed, so is your seat of power"
    The town hall building can be broken or razed by enemies. Protect it, and once you can, wall it in. A settlement without its hall has lost its command center.

## Raise the banner: your public standard

Founding lights the campfire, but a settlement is truly **bound** to a second object: its **faction banner**, also called your **public standard**. When you found, the game tries to plant this banner on clear ground beside the campfire automatically. If there is no open spot, you will be told to **place one from your supplies nearby** yourself.

The rule to remember:

!!! warning "The 24-block rule"
    Your faction banner must stand **within 24 blocks of the town hall**, on ground with a **clear access side** — an open, reachable face beside it. Keep it standing, keep it reachable.

Why does this matter so much? Because the banner is what holds your settlement together:

- **Banner up:** *"The banner flies! The settlement is bound to it."* Your menus work, your citizens work, your civilization runs.
- **Banner down:** the settlement is **unbound** — **no menus, no work** — until the banner is raised again.

If the banner is knocked down, broken, or ends up with no clear side to path to, everything freezes. Your town hall will tell you plainly: *"Raise the official banner within 24 blocks of the town hall with a clear access side to restore command."* Plant a banner back in your territory and command returns.

!!! tip "Treat the banner like a heartbeat"
    In peacetime, tuck your public standard somewhere safe and open near the hall — not on a cliff edge, not buried in a wall, not boxed in where no one can reach it. In war, enemies will target it deliberately, because toppling your standard cripples you far faster than fighting your citizens one by one.

## Where you are allowed to settle

You cannot found a settlement anywhere you please. Two rules keep civilizations from crowding each other:

- [x] **At least 6 chunks from any other settlement.** Your campfire must sit a minimum of **6 chunks away** from another settlement's **claimed chunks**. Too close, and you will be told to move farther out.
- [x] **Never on or beside a city-state.** You cannot settle **on or next to a city-state's territory**. These older powers hold their ground — approach them through [diplomacy](../civilization/diplomacy.md), not by squatting on their doorstep.
- [x] **Your banner color must be free.** As above, no two settlements share a color.

!!! example "Scouting a founding site"
    You place your unlit campfire, right-click, and get *"Too close to another settlement — move at least 6 chunks away."* Pick the campfire back up, walk out a chunk or two, and try again. Repeat until the Site Assessment appears instead of the warning — that means the spot is legally clear, and now you only have to judge whether the *land* is good.

## Settlement stages

A new settlement begins tiny and grows through named **stages** as its population and progress climb. You start at the humblest and rise:

| Stage | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Hearth** | A single fire and a founder — the very beginning. |
| **Tribe** | A band of citizens working together. |
| **Village** | A settled community with real infrastructure. |
| **Town** | A larger, established settlement. |
| **City** | A true power on the map. |

Advancing through these stages unlocks new abilities and reflects how far your people have come. Your current stage is shown in your town hall, and climbing it is one of the quiet measures of a leader's success.

## Inviting other players

Bannerbound is meant to be shared. Two documents let you bring **other players** into your settlement as fellow members, and you issue both from the town hall.

Registration Tablet
:   A **Registration Tablet** is your main invitation. Issue one from the town hall's **Get Registration Tablet** button — under a Chiefdom the **Chief** issues it directly, while a Council puts it to a settlement vote first. Once issued, hand the tablet to the player you want to recruit; they **right-click** it to join, and its tooltip reads *"Right-click to join &lt;your settlement&gt;."*

Registration Paper
:   A **Registration Paper** is a **lighter invitation** used by the same registration flow — a simpler way to bring someone aboard.

!!! info "Each era raises your limit"
    Your settlement can only issue so many registration documents at once, and the **Get Registration Tablet** button shows your current count against that cap. Early on the number is small — try to issue one too many and you will be told your settlement *"has already issued a Registration Tablet. (Future research will unlock more.)"* **Each era you advance into raises how many you can issue**, so a young Hearth invites cautiously while a sprawling City can welcome many.

### Joining a settlement

To join, a player simply **right-clicks a Registration Tablet or Paper** that was issued for that settlement. A couple of things to know:

- You can only belong to **one settlement at a time**. If you are already in one, you must **leave it first** before you can join another.
- A tablet is only useful once it has been issued for a specific settlement — a **blank** tablet does nothing until a town hall stamps it.

## Leaving a settlement

Leaving is done through the town hall's **Leave Settlement** option, but it is not instant, and it is not always allowed:

!!! warning "Two catches when you leave"
    - **Fresh-settler cooldown.** Right after you settle or join, you **cannot leave for a short while** — *"you just settled in."* This stops churn and settlement-hopping. Wait out the cooldown and the option opens up.
    - **Chiefs must step down first.** If you lead a [Chiefdom](../civilization/government.md), you **cannot walk away while you are Chief** — step down first, then leave. A settlement is never abandoned headless.

Once the cooldown has passed and you hold no office you must resign, leaving takes you back to the life of a wandering settler — free to found somewhere new, or accept a tablet into someone else's story.
