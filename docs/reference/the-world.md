# How the World Changes

<p class="bb-lead">The land you spawn into looks like Minecraft — the same biomes, the same mountains, the same caves winding under your feet. But it does not play like it. Almost everything a vanilla explorer sets out to find has been quietly taken away, and something else put in its place. This page is here so you don't waste a week hiking to loot that no longer exists. Here's what the world no longer holds — and what it holds instead.</p>

!!! abstract "The short version"
    Bannerbound turns off vanilla's whole treasure-hunt layer. **Villages**, **mineshafts**,
    **strongholds**, **ocean monuments**, **woodland mansions**, **pillager outposts**, and
    **ruined portals** no longer generate, and **dungeons** (monster-spawner rooms) are stripped out.
    **Ore** hides invisibly inside ordinary stone until your people learn to see it. The old ways into
    the **Nether** and the **End** are sealed. Don't go treasure-hunting — the reward loop is your
    settlement, not a chest in a cave. See [Founding a Settlement](../getting-started/founding-a-settlement.md).

## The structures you won't find

Every vanilla overworld structure set ships empty in Bannerbound, so **none of them generate anywhere**.
There is no village on the horizon, no stronghold beneath your feet, no mansion in the dark forest. The
world is genuinely wild — the only settlements in it are the ones players and city-states build.

| Structure | What it gave you in vanilla | In Bannerbound |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Villages** | Villagers to trade with, free beds and crops, a head start | Gone. You **found and grow your own** settlement instead — see [Settlements](../civilization/settlements.md) |
| **Mineshafts** | Rails, chest loot, and long corridors of exposed ore | Gone. Ore isn't exposed anywhere (see below); a **Miner** works veins you reveal |
| **Strongholds** | The End portal room and a library of enchanting | Gone. The End is sealed regardless |
| **Ocean monuments** | Guardians, sponges, prismarine, treasure | Gone |
| **Woodland mansions** | Totems of undying, illager loot | Gone |
| **Pillager outposts** | Captain raids and crossbow loot | Gone. The raiders you'll actually meet are **barbarian camps** — see [Barbarians](../civilization/barbarians.md) |
| **Ruined portals** | Free obsidian frames and chest loot | Gone. Portals are sealed anyway |

!!! note "Barbarian camps replace the pillager threat"
    You will still be raided — but by **barbarians**, not pillagers, and their camps are placed by
    Bannerbound's own systems as physical settlements (a campfire and a colored standard ringed by huts),
    not by vanilla worldgen. They scale to your world's technology and fight back with thrown spears and
    bows. The whole story lives on the [Barbarians](../civilization/barbarians.md) page.

## No dungeons, no monster rooms

Vanilla's cobblestone **dungeons** — the little mossy rooms built around a mob spawner, with a chest or
two of loot — are removed from underground generation throughout the overworld. There are no spawner
rooms to farm and no dungeon chests to raid. Caves are just caves: rock, water, and whatever you dig for.

## The ore is there — you just can't see it yet

This is the change that catches new players hardest. In Bannerbound, **ore is hidden inside ordinary
stone.** Strip-mine to bedrock on your first day and you'll find nothing but stone, no matter how far
you dig — the veins are there, but your people don't yet know what one looks like.

Ore becomes visible only once you **research** it. Early prospecting research teaches your settlement to
read the rock — the rust of iron, the green of copper, the dull glint of tin — and only then do those
veins appear in the stone for a **Miner** to work. And unlike vanilla, a revealed vein **renews as it's
mined** rather than running dry, so a claimed deposit keeps paying out.

!!! tip "So how do I get metal?"
    You research your way to it, then put a citizen to work. The full path — which research reveals
    which ore, and how the Miner (and the outpost workers who reach beyond your border) pull it out — is
    on [Research & Eras](../civilization/research-and-eras.md) and [Jobs & Labor](../civilization/jobs-and-labor.md).
    Rich deposits are tied to **biome and territory**, so where you settle matters; see
    [Territory & Outposts](../civilization/territory.md).

## The Nether and the End are sealed

The ancient roads out of the overworld are shut. Strike **flint and steel** against a finished obsidian
frame and nothing lights; seat an **eye of ender** into an End portal frame and it won't take. Either
way the game tells you plainly:

!!! warning "The old ways are closed"
    *"The old ways into the Nether and the End are closed to you."*

    There is no Nether to visit for blaze rods or quartz, and no End to reach for elytra or shulkers —
    those horizons are reserved for a far later age of your civilization, long past the stone-age start
    this pack begins in. Don't stockpile obsidian or ender eyes expecting a portal; they won't open.

Operators and creative-mode players can still force a portal for testing — see the admin notes on
[Keybinds & Commands](keybinds-and-commands.md) — but in ordinary survival, both dimensions stay behind
you.

## The ruins of the fallen

Here's the one thing the world *gains*. When a settlement is **razed** in war or **disbanded** by its
people, it isn't deleted in a blink. Instead it **slowly crumbles into ruin.** Building by building, the
walls and roofs and towers peel back down toward the natural lie of the land, until all that's left is a
scatter of rubble on the grade where a town once stood. Its villagers are gone; its trees are left
standing; and if a new settlement ever reclaims those chunks, the crumbling stops and the land is theirs.

For an explorer, that means the wilderness holds a quiet kind of history. Wander far enough and you may
come across the **decaying remains of a fallen colony or a broken city-state** — not a designed dungeon,
but the real, weathering footprint of somewhere that failed. It won't be full of loot. It's a marker of
what happened here, and a hint of who your neighbors used to be. City-states and the wars that end them
are covered on [Diplomacy](../civilization/diplomacy.md).

!!! example "What a ruin looks like"
    A razed capital doesn't vanish — over time its stone streets slump into low mounds, its houses
    collapse to ankle-height rubble, and the ground reasserts itself. Come back to a place you once
    fought over and you'll find it half-swallowed by the world, an uneven ruin-line where the skyline
    used to be.
