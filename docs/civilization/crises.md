# Crises

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Sooner or later, the fires fall quiet and every face turns to you. A crisis is the moment your settlement stops running on its own and waits for the Chief to decide.</em></p>

!!! abstract "In short"
    A **crisis** is a settlement-wide emergency that surfaces as a **Crisis** notification and a full-screen decision. Until someone chooses, the settlement sits in *"Waiting for the decision."* Members can **advise** the Chief, but the leader makes the binding call. A crisis drags everyone's mood down while it runs; resolving it lifts the whole settlement and repairs your standing. The first one you will face is **Starvation** — answer it by putting people to work making food, not by hoping your wandering stores hold out.

## What a crisis actually is

Most of the time your settlement hums along in the background — citizens eat, sleep, work their [jobs](jobs-and-labor.md), and grumble or cheer on their own. A crisis breaks that rhythm. It is a scripted turning point that rises out of a real problem: too many hungry mouths, too many people sleeping under open sky, a threat at the gates.

A crisis is not a punishment. It exists to make an important system *impossible to ignore* — to take a slow, easy-to-forget pressure like "we should probably set up steady food" and put it front and centre with a name, a headline, and a clock. Answer one well and your settlement comes out the other side stronger than it went in.

!!! quote "From the Chronicle"
    *"Crises are not there to punish you — they exist to make important systems impossible to ignore. Answering one well leaves your settlement stronger than before it began."*

Each crisis is written into the **Chronicle**, your in-game guidebook. The Chronicle holds the permanent knowledge; while a crisis is live, the **side objective tracker** on your screen holds the running task list for the path you chose.

!!! note "One at a time, and only once"
    A settlement faces **one crisis at a time** — a new one can't fire while another still waits for an answer. And each named crisis fires **only once**: settle Starvation and it won't return, even though citizens can still go hungry later through ordinary [food](food.md) shortfalls. "Preventing the next one" always means a *different* crisis, never a repeat of one you've already resolved.

## Crises you may face

These are the emergencies defined for the early game. Both wait until you have a [government](government.md) — a wandering band with no leader has no one to answer them.

| Crisis | What sets it off | How you answer it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Starvation** | Fires right after you enact your first government, when the old wandering stores can no longer feed the tribe. | Stand up steady food — pick **Fishers**, **Farmers & Fields**, or **Herders & Husbandry**. |
| **A Roof Over Their Heads** | Fires once **4 citizens** are homeless and stay that way for about **5 minutes** running. | Research **Housing** and raise a proper home for every citizen. |

Both leave your settlement permanently better off: a food engine, or homes for all. Each is walked through in full below.

## How a crisis surfaces

When the conditions for a crisis are met, three things happen at once:

- A **Crisis: _<name>_** notification announces it to the settlement.
- A dramatic full-screen **decision** opens — a headline, a plea from your people, and a short list of choices, each with its own consequences.
- Until a decision is made, the settlement is marked **"Waiting for the decision."** Life does not simply carry on as if nothing is wrong.

Closed the popup without answering? You haven't missed your chance. For as long as a choice is pending, a floating **alert marker** hovers over your **Town Hall** for everyone nearby to see, and **right-clicking the Town Hall reopens the decision screen** in place of the usual menu — so a member who logged in partway through the crisis, or anyone who dismissed the popup, knows exactly where to go to answer.

The decision screen lays out every option with a description of what it commits you to. Some options also show a **viability warning** when your settlement can't support that path yet — for example, *"No claimed pond or shore looks fishable yet"* or *"No tillable dirt or grass is claimed."* You can still pick a warned path, but you'll have work to do before it can succeed.

## Making the decision

Who gets to answer a crisis depends on your form of [government](government.md).

=== "Chiefdom"

    The **Chief** makes the binding decision — full stop. Ordinary members cannot resolve the crisis themselves.

    What members *can* do is **advise**. When a member picks an option on the decision screen, it is recorded as a non-binding tally the Chief can see before choosing, and they get the confirmation *"You advised the chief to: _<choice>_."* Advice is a live vote count that refreshes on every open screen — a way for the settlement to voice which way it's leaning, while the final word stays with the leader.

=== "Council"

    A council answers together. A choice only takes effect once **enough members** back the same option — each vote is tallied, and the decision is enacted the moment one option clears the council's threshold. Until then, the crisis keeps waiting.

!!! tip "Let your people advise"
    Even under a chiefdom, encourage members to weigh in. The advice tally is a fast read on morale and consensus — if the settlement is unanimously begging for one answer, overruling it is a choice you should make deliberately, not by accident.

## Crises weigh on everyone's mood

A crisis doesn't just wait politely. While it runs, it presses on the whole settlement: citizens carry the thought *"The settlement is in crisis"*, and their [mood](citizens.md) sags for as long as the emergency drags on. An unanswered crisis is a slow, steady drain on happiness across every home and workshop.

Resolving it flips that. When a crisis is settled, you'll see **Crisis resolved: _<name>_**, and citizens pick up the thought *"Relieved after the crisis"* — a settlement-wide sigh of relief that lifts mood back up. Resolving well also repairs your standing as leader: your people grow more willing to follow, and their resentment cools.

!!! warning "Don't let a crisis linger"
    The longer a crisis sits in *"Waiting for the decision,"* the longer everyone stews. Decide promptly. A fast, decent answer usually beats a slow, perfect one — especially when unhappy citizens can spiral into problems of their own.

## Worked example: the Starvation crisis

Your first crisis is almost always **Starvation**, and it arrives right after you enact your first government. The message is blunt: *"The people are starving!"* Your tribe has outgrown the lucky handfuls of food it gathered while wandering, and now it demands that you *make* food by design.

The decision offers three paths — **Fishers**, **Farmers & Fields**, and **Herders & Husbandry**. They differ in flavor and setup, but every one of them is solved the same way underneath: **create a stable rate of stored food that can feed a full tribe.** The crisis measures *stored valid food* — catches, harvests, and meat sitting in your claimed storage, safe from spoilage and poison — not what's loose in your backpack.

!!! danger "Ignore the old 'drop-off chest' step"
    Older guidance told you to *"mark a drop-off chest"* for your food workers. **That is no longer how it works.** Your workers now deliver to any claimed storage inside your [territory](territory.md) automatically — there is no chest to designate. If your gatherers seem to do nothing, the cause is almost always one of the four things below, not a missing drop-off.

### The four things every food path needs

Whichever path you choose, the fix is the same shape. Get these four right and the stored-food rate climbs on its own:

- [ ] **Assign the job.** Open a citizen's **Job** tab and assign the gatherer the path calls for — Spear Fisher, Farmer, or Herder. Aim for at least two workers on it.
- [ ] **Set the work area.** Give the job somewhere to work. Bind a field or a fenced pen with the **Foreman's Rod**; for fishers, claim a good stretch of fishable water. Without a work area, a gatherer has nowhere to gather.
- [ ] **Provide the tool.** Put the right tool where the worker can reach it — a spear for fishers, a hoe for farmers, [**fiber rope**](jobs-and-labor.md) for herders — in their tool slot or your shared tool storage. (A spear fisher will wade in bare-handed, but a spear makes them far faster; the herder objective, though, counts *only* herders actually holding their rope.)
- [ ] **Keep storage in claimed chunks.** Your **baskets** and other containers must sit inside your **claimed [territory](territory.md)**. Food delivered to claimed storage counts toward the crisis; food dropped outside your claim does not.

Each path also begins with a bit of [research](research-and-eras.md) from the **Town Hall** research screen before the job is available:

=== "Fishers"

    *"We'll feed through fish."* Wade the shallows and strike at what swims below — the fastest path if you've claimed water.

    1. Research **Spear Fishing** at the Town Hall.
    2. Assign two **Spear Fishers** in a citizen's Job tab.
    3. Claim a fishable pond or shoreline as their water, and hand them a **spear**.
    4. Keep your baskets in claimed chunks near the water.

    !!! warning "Viability"
        If the decision screen warns *"No claimed pond or shore looks fishable yet,"* claim some fishable water before committing — or the fishers will have nothing to strike at.

=== "Farmers & Fields"

    *"We'll feed through fields."* Break the soil, save seed, and turn hunger into planned harvests — slower to start, but scales well.

    1. Research **Agricultural Revolution** at the Town Hall.
    2. Assign two **Farmers** in a citizen's Job tab.
    3. Bind a field with the **Foreman's Rod** and give the farmers **hoes**.
    4. Keep your baskets in claimed chunks near the field.

    !!! tip "Raw wheat isn't enough"
        Wheat only counts once it's food — grow edible crops or bake it into bread. See [Food](food.md) for what the settlement will actually eat.

    !!! warning "Viability"
        If the screen warns *"No tillable dirt or grass is claimed,"* claim some workable soil before you commit to fields.

=== "Herders & Husbandry"

    *"We'll feed through the animals."* Pens, breeding stock, and patience turn wandering beasts into a living reserve.

    1. Research **Herding** and then **Animal Husbandry** at the Town Hall.
    2. Assign two **Herders** in a citizen's Job tab, and hand each one **fiber rope** — the "appoint 2 herders" objective only counts herders holding their rope.
    3. Fence a pen, bind it with the **Foreman's Rod**, and keep breeding stock inside.
    4. Cull surplus animals into baskets kept in claimed chunks.

    !!! warning "Viability"
        If the screen warns *"No claimed grassland is ready for animals,"* claim some grazing land before you commit to the herd.

### Knowing you've won

Watch the food line in your **Town Hall**. Hover it to see exactly how much food per second your **stored food** is contributing. The Starvation crisis clears once your settlement's total food production meets or beats what a *full* tribe eats — a whole settlement's worth of mouths, even if a hard season has temporarily thinned your numbers.

Your chosen path leads the way, but **every food worker counts** toward that rate. If fishing alone falls short, add fishers, claim better spots, or put a few more citizens on farming or herding until the line holds above what a full tribe needs.

!!! success "Coming out ahead"
    Resolve Starvation and you don't just survive — you finish with a permanent food engine, a couple of trained gatherers, and a settlement that's relieved rather than resentful. That's the whole point of a crisis.

## Worked example: the Housing crisis

The second emergency you're likely to meet is **A Roof Over Their Heads**, which fires once **four citizens** are left homeless and stay that way for roughly **five minutes** running. The plea is pointed: *"The people demand homes!"* A person with no door of their own feels they belong to no one — and they won't labor willingly much longer without shelter.

This crisis has a single path: **build**. Resolving it means unlocking the craft of building and putting a roof over every head.

1. Research **Housing** at the **Town Hall** research screen. This unlocks **Housing Orders** and thatch building parts.
2. Use **Housing Orders** to plan homes — each needs a roof, a door, a window, and a bed of its own.
3. Build until **every citizen has an assigned home**. See [Housing](housing.md) for how homes and beds are claimed.

!!! warning "Homeless citizens sour fast"
    The longer someone waits without a home, the unhappier they grow. Prioritise the citizens who've been sleeping rough the longest, and the crisis eases as the last roof goes up.

## Preventing the next one

The best-handled crisis is the one that never fires. A little foresight keeps the fires quiet:

- **Build a food buffer.** Keep stored food comfortably ahead of what your people eat, so a bad season is a dip, not a disaster. See [Food](food.md).
- **Keep citizens happy.** A content settlement absorbs shocks; a miserable one tips into trouble at the first sign of strain. Watch [citizen](citizens.md) mood and address complaints early.
- **Post guards and raise walls.** Not every crisis is about food. Standing defenders and good [walls](walls.md) keep [barbarian](barbarians.md) raids from becoming emergencies.
- **Build homes ahead of demand.** The Housing crisis brews quietly whenever too many citizens sleep in the open — raise [homes](housing.md) before the grumbling turns to anger.

!!! tip "Treat crises as a checklist for the future"
    Every crisis points at a system worth mastering *before* it's urgent. Once you've answered Starvation, you know how to run steady food; once you've answered the Housing crisis, you know how to shelter a growing town. Keep those engines humming and the next crisis will find you ready.
