# Culture & Heraldry

<p class="bb-lead"><em>A civilization is more than fed mouths and stacked stone. It is a look, a colour, a banner your people would follow into a storm, and a tongue only they speak. These are the systems that make your settlement <strong>yours</strong> — the taste it builds by, the flag it flies, and the words it invents along the way.</em></p>

!!! abstract "In short"
    - At founding you choose a **Culture Style** — the aesthetic your people build by. It quietly decides which blocks they find *beautiful* and which they find ugly.
    - **Culture** is a resource, generated per claimed chunk, that fuels the **Culture Research** tree.
    - **Palettes** are research-unlocked sets of blocks that grant an appeal bonus. They fill a few limited slots and are switched on by a settlement **vote**, like a policy — from the town hall **Palettes** tab.
    - **Heraldry** unlocks the **faction banner editor**: layer patterns over a base colour, spending **Heraldry points** you earn from Culture research. The banner's three most-shown colours become your settlement's **Primary**, **Accent**, and **Trim** — the colours of every menu.
    - Your settlement slowly coins its own words. Read them in the town hall **Lexicon** tab.

---

## Culture, the resource

**Culture** is one of the currents your settlement produces just by existing on the land. Every chunk you claim contributes a small trickle of **Culture per second**, shown on that chunk's line when you open the [Territory](territory.md) map — a beautiful, well-placed chunk is worth more than a barren one. That trickle pools into a stockpile you spend in the **Culture Research** tree. A few [faith](faith.md) blessings can add to your Culture income as well.

Culture research is the softer half of [Research & Eras](research-and-eras.md). Where the science tree hands you tools and jobs, Culture research shapes how your people express themselves — it unlocks **Palettes**, opens the **Heraldry** editor, and grants the **Heraldry points** you spend on your banner. A flourishing culture also lifts citizen spirits: contentment with your settlement's identity is one of the threads woven into overall [happiness](citizens.md).

!!! tip "Beauty is currency"
    Because per-chunk Culture scales with how appealing the land is, the same blocks that keep your citizens happy also earn you Culture faster. Building well isn't just decoration — it pays for your entire cultural future.

## Choosing a Culture Style

When you [found a settlement](../getting-started/founding-a-settlement.md), the founding screen walks you through a name, a banner colour, and — on the **Choose a Culture Style** step — the aesthetic your people will build by.

A Culture Style is your settlement's inborn *taste*. It reshapes how appealing your citizens find hundreds of different blocks: a **Forest** people adore moss, ferns, cobblestone, timber and open water, and turn up their noses at sand, terracotta and mycelium. Those preferences flow straight into [housing appeal](housing.md) and territory beauty, so your Style nudges what a *pleasant* build looks like for the rest of the game.

!!! warning "It's chosen once"
    You pick your Culture Style at founding and live with it. Choose the one that matches the land you're settling and the palette you want to build in — a Desert people planted in a snowfield will find very little around them beautiful.

The Styles on offer are drawn from the world's biomes:

| Style | Builds beautifully in… |
| --- | --- |
| **Forest** | Oak and birch timber, moss, cobblestone, ferns, wildflowers, open water |
| **Birch** | Bright birch woodland — pale planks, leaves and gentle greenery |
| **Dark Oak** | Deep, shadowed groves of dark oak and rich earth |
| **Jungle** | Dense jungle timber, vines, leaves and humid greenery |
| **Taiga** | Spruce forest, podzol, ferns and cool stone |
| **Tundra** | Snow, ice and hardy frostbitten builds |
| **Savanna** | Acacia, dry grass and warm earthen tones |
| **Desert** | Sandstone, terracotta and sun-baked construction |
| **Mountainside** | Stone, tuff, andesite and highland masonry |
| **Waterside** | Rivers, lakes, lily pads and lush shoreline living |

!!! example "What a Style actually changes"
    Under the **Forest** Style, moss and mossy cobblestone are prized, timber and worked stone are welcome, and open water is one of the most beautiful things your people can live beside — while cactus, red sand and mycelium read as eyesores. Two settlements can stand on identical ground and disagree completely about whether it's lovely, because their Cultures were founded differently.

## Palettes

As you push into the Culture tree you unlock **Palettes** — curated sets of blocks that your people have learned to love. While a Palette is active, every block in it grants a bonus to its appeal wherever it's placed in your territory, stacking on top of your Culture Style's baseline taste. Palettes are how you steer your settlement's look *after* founding, rewarding a committed architectural theme with happier citizens and more beautiful chunks.

??? note "Two layers of taste"
    Your Style and your Palettes are different tools that stack on the same appeal score:

    === "Culture Style"
        - Chosen **once**, at founding.
        - Covers **hundreds** of blocks with both bonuses *and* penalties.
        - Permanent — it's the deep grain of your people's taste.

    === "Palettes"
        - **Researched**, then swapped in and out over the life of the settlement.
        - Each favours a **handful** of blocks with a bonus only (no penalties).
        - Enacted by a vote, limited to a few active slots at a time.

You manage them from the town hall's **Palettes** tab. It has two lists: **Active palettes** (the ones currently in effect, filling a small number of limited slots — an unused slot reads *"Empty slot"*) and **Available Palettes** (everything your research has unlocked, each block shown with its bonus, e.g. *"Moss Block  +0.20"*). Until you research one, the tab simply reads *"Research unlocks palettes."*

### Enacting a Palette

Switching a Palette on or off is a civic act, handled like a [policy](government.md):

- [ ] The **Chief** proposes the change and confirms it to open the ballot — *(only the Chief may change palettes)*.
- [ ] The settlement **votes**: each citizen may **Agree** or **Disagree**, and the tally updates live — *"Voted 3/5 — 2 agree"* — as your people weigh in.
- [ ] If enough agree, it's enacted and *"A palette has changed."* If not, *"the palette vote did not pass."*

Slots are limited, so Palettes compete for space. If every slot is full you'll be told *"No free palette slot — remove one first"* — swap an old theme out before voting a new one in. Only one palette change can be on the ballot at a time.

### The unlockable Palettes

Each Palette leans into a distinct building material or mood. Every block it favours gains a modest appeal boost — roughly **+0.10 to +0.25** appeal per block — while the Palette is active:

| Palette | Its favoured blocks |
| --- | --- |
| **Forest Hollow** | Oak & spruce logs, moss, ferns, oak & spruce leaves |
| **Cherry Garden** | Cherry log & planks, cherry leaves, pink petals, flowering azalea |
| **Brown Haven** | Dirt, coarse & rooted dirt, oak & dark oak logs |
| **Copper Quarter** | Copper block, cut copper, and its exposed, weathered & oxidized forms |
| **Desert Caravan** | Sandstone, smooth & cut sandstone, terracotta, orange terracotta |
| **Pale Stonework** | Stone bricks, polished diorite, quartz block, smooth stone |
| **Frosthold** | Snow block, packed & blue ice, spruce & stripped spruce logs |
| **Blackstone Bastion** | Polished blackstone & bricks, gilded blackstone, deepslate tiles, chiseled blackstone |

!!! tip "Match your Palette to your Style"
    A Palette layered on top of a complementary Culture Style compounds: a Forest people running **Forest Hollow** turn a mossy timber town into a genuine happiness engine. A mismatched Palette still helps, but you're fighting your own founding taste.

## Heraldry: your faction banner

Your banner is your settlement's face. It flies over the town hall, it marks your people in [war and diplomacy](diplomacy.md), and its colours paint every menu you open. When you research **Heraldry** in the Culture tree, a **Banner** button appears on the town hall, opening the **Faction Banner** editor.

### Designing the banner

The editor is a proper heraldic workshop. On the left is your **Layers** list; in the middle, a live banner waving in real time; on the right, a grid of patterns and the sixteen dyes.

- Your banner starts as a solid **base colour** (the one you chose at founding).
- Press **Add Layer** to lay a pattern over it, then pick the pattern and its dye. Reorder layers with the ▲▼ arrows, remove one with ✕, and click a layer to edit it.
- You can stack up to **six** pattern layers over the base.

Layers aren't free — they cost **Heraldry points**. Each pattern layer occupies **one** earned point for as long as it exists, and removing a layer gives the point back. You start with few, and *Culture researches grant more Heraldry points* — so a richer banner is a reward for investing in your culture. The tab tracks your total as **Heraldry** with a row of point pips, and if you reach for more layers than you can afford it warns *"Not enough Heraldry points for that many layers."*

Press **Save** to seal the design: *"the faction banner design is sealed — the flag and every future banner bear it."*

!!! note "Locked until researched"
    Open the editor before finishing the Heraldry research and it simply reports *"Heraldry has not been researched."* And only someone with authority over the settlement may change the faction banner — everyone else is told they lack it.

### Your identity colours

Here's the clever part: your banner doesn't just *look* like your settlement — it *defines* its colours. When you save, the editor tallies how much of each dye actually shows on the flag — the base colour fills the whole banner, and every pattern claims a share in its own colour, proportionally hiding what's beneath it — then ranks them:

| Rank | Becomes your… | Where you see it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Most-shown colour | **Primary** | Your settlement's core identity colour |
| Runner-up | **Accent** | Trim and gradients across your menus |
| Third | **Trim** | Completes your menus' gradients |

The editor shows these as live **Identity** swatches while you work, so you can watch your settlement's whole colour scheme shift as you paint. Want a crimson-and-gold civilization? Make sure red shows most and gold second, and every window you open afterwards will wear those colours.

!!! info "A colour has to earn its place"
    A dye only counts toward your identity once it covers enough of the cloth. A bold two-tone banner may resolve to just a Primary and an Accent, while a busy, many-coloured flag can fill all three slots — so design with the scheme you want in mind.

### Taking a copy

Proud of the design? Use **Take a copy** to pull a decorative banner into your hands — *"plant it anywhere for decoration."* It isn't free: a copy costs **one of each dye in the design** (base plus every pattern), drawn from your settlement's storage. If the dyes aren't in the stockpile, the editor tells you exactly which ones are missing.

!!! warning "The banner must fly"
    The faction banner is not just cosmetic — your settlement is *bound* to it. While it stands, your town hall and every job keep working; if it's taken down or destroyed, *"the settlement is unbound — no menus, no work — until it is raised again."* To restore command, raise the official banner **within 24 blocks of the town hall** with a clear, reachable access side. See [Founding a Settlement](../getting-started/founding-a-settlement.md) for raising it in the first place.

## The Settlement Lexicon

Over time, your people stop borrowing words and start coining their own. Every settlement speaks a subtly different tongue, seeded from its name and its founding — and as it grows and advances through the eras, it invents names for the concepts that matter to it. You can browse this living dictionary in the town hall's **Lexicon** tab.

The **Settlement Lexicon** is your people's growing word-list, shown with a running count (*"… words"*) and a **Search words…** box to hunt through them by invented word, meaning, or category. A young settlement starts nearly wordless — *"No words recorded yet"* — and fills its dictionary as it works, researches, and ages. Every row pairs the **invented word** with its plain-English **meaning** and, where one exists, the shared **root** it grew from — so you can spot when two words spring from the same stem. The rows are colour-coded by which corner of settlement life they name — **Goods** in blue, **Work** in orange, **Knowledge** in green, **Tradition** in purple:

| Category | Names… | Grows as you… |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Goods** | Every item your settlement knows | Discover and unlock new goods |
| **Work** | The jobs your people hold | Unlock new professions |
| **Knowledge** | Completed **science** research | Finish nodes in the [science tree](research-and-eras.md) |
| **Tradition** | Completed **Culture** research | Finish nodes in the Culture tree |

So the Lexicon isn't a fixed vocabulary — it thickens in step with your civilization. A fresh Antiquity village has words for little more than its first goods and gatherers; a mature one names hundreds of items, every trade it practises, and every idea it has ever researched. The coined forms lean on your current era, too, so a word's flavour can shift as your people advance.

This isn't just flavour text on a shelf. The same tongue styles your [citizens'](citizens.md) own **names**: a person's given name is spoken in your settlement's language from birth, and the **surnames** they earn through their trade grow from the very same roots the Lexicon records — a hunter's family name and the settlement's word for *"Hunter"* share a stem. No two civilizations will ever sound quite alike.

Citizen names always speak the settlement tongue — but the language can spread much further, if you let it. With the **`useCustomLanguage`** game rule switched on, your people's words replace the ordinary English labels *across the whole interface*, not just the Lexicon tab: a known item wears its coined name everywhere it names itself (the tooltip's first line, the hotbar selector, the anvil, chat hover), job titles read in your tongue on every citizen and roster screen, and even the creatures around you answer to your settlement's names. It's off by default; a server owner turns it on with `/gamerule useCustomLanguage true`. See [Multiplayer](../reference/multiplayer.md) for this rule and the others.

!!! quote "A tongue of your own"
    Two towns can be founded a hundred blocks apart, on the same biome, with the same Culture Style — and still name the world differently. The Lexicon is where that difference is written down.
