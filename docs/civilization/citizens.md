# Citizens & Needs

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Every settlement is people first. Your citizens eat, sleep, argue, fall in love, raise children, master their crafts — and, if you neglect them, refuse your orders and choose a new leader. Keep them fed, housed, and heard, and they will build you an empire.</em></p>

!!! abstract "The short version"
    - Happiness rests on **four equal pillars** — **Food**, **Culture**, **Comfort**, and **Society**. Your overall mood is the average of the four.
    - That mood lands in one of three **bands**: **Content** (a green all-round bonus), **Indifferent** (no change), or **Miserable** (a heavy penalty to work and movement).
    - **Thoughts** are the little lines behind the mood — a loved home, a good meal, a lost friend. Neglected grievances like homelessness and hunger **deepen the longer you ignore them**.
    - Workers climb five **skill tiers** and grow **stamina**, **health**, and — crucially — **compliance**. Let compliance collapse and workers **strike**; let unrest fester and the people **depose your chief at dawn**.

**Right-click** any citizen to open their screen. Along the top you'll find four tabs — **Info**, **Relations**, **Thoughts**, and **Job**. The **Info** tab reads out their **Health**, **Happiness**, **Stamina**, **Compliance**, and **Resentment**; the **Thoughts** tab breaks that mood down into its four pillars. This page is a tour of every one of those readings and what moves them.

---

## The four pillars of happiness

Happiness isn't a single bar. Each citizen keeps four separate scores — one per pillar, each running 0–100 — and your headline **Happiness** is simply the average of the four equal pillars. The **Thoughts** tab shows them as four filling rings, each with its own percentage, so you can tell at a glance *why* someone is glum: a fed, housed worker with no friends and no beauty around them is a very different problem from a starving one. Hover any ring to see exactly which thoughts are feeding that pillar.

| Pillar | :material-circle: Colour | What feeds it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Food** | Green | Nourishment and meals — going hungry, eating well, variety and drink |
| **Culture** | Purple | Beauty, family and identity — settlement [appeal](culture-and-heraldry.md), children, [research](research-and-eras.md), [faith](faith.md) |
| **Comfort** | Blue | Shelter, health and safety — [home](housing.md) quality, injury, weather, [crises](crises.md) |
| **Society** | Amber | Work, governance and relationships — employment, [policies](government.md), friends |

!!! tip "Balance beats brute force"
    Because the pillars are averaged, a citizen who is *superb* on one pillar and *starving* on another still ends up unhappy. It is usually cheaper to lift a neglected pillar from bad to okay than to gild one that is already full. If a whole district sours at once, look for the shared cause: an empty granary, a razed forest, a housing shortage.

## Happiness bands

Wherever the average lands, it falls into one of three bands. The band is what the world actually *feels* — it changes how fast citizens move, how quickly they work, and how fast skilled workers improve. On the **Info** tab, the Happiness bar itself is split red / yellow / green, with a marker sliding along it to show exactly where this citizen sits.

| Band | Mood | Movement | Work speed | Experience |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| :material-emoticon-happy: **Content** | Green | **+15%** | **+15%** | **+15%** |
| :material-emoticon-neutral: **Indifferent** | Yellow | — | — | — |
| :material-emoticon-sad: **Miserable** | Red | **−10%** | **−30%** | **−30%** |

The gap between the top and bottom band is enormous — a Content workforce out-produces a Miserable one by a wide margin, and skilled crafters level up far faster while the mood holds. Happiness is not decoration; it is one of your strongest levers on output.

!!! warning "Misery snowballs"
    A Miserable citizen works and learns slowly, which means less food, less building, and less of everything that would have made them happy — a downward spiral. Worse, sustained misery is what erodes **compliance** and eventually drives **strikes** and **coups** (below). Catch a settlement-wide slump early.

## Thoughts: why they feel that way

The **Thoughts** tab lists the specific lines pushing a citizen's mood up or down right now — "I love my house!", "I enjoyed a good meal.", "A close friend of mine died recently." Each thought belongs to one of the four pillars and carries it. Most fade on their own after a while; a few last only until the situation changes.

**Some grievances get worse the longer you ignore them.** A freshly homeless, jobless, or starving citizen starts with a mild complaint — but if you leave it unaddressed, that same thought **deepens day by day** into a serious drag on happiness. Hunger sours fastest of all. Address the root cause promptly and the thought resets.

Here is the flavour of what you'll see, grouped by pillar.

=== "Food"

    | Thought | Mood |
    | --- | --- |
    | "I'm eating very well." | Big lift |
    | "I'm eating well." | Small lift |
    | "I enjoyed a good meal." | Small lift |
    | "I went to bed hungry." | Small hit |
    | "I was starving recently." | Small hit |
    | "I'm starving!" | Severe — and deepens while it lasts |

    Keep the granary ahead of the population, and cook real meals rather than handing out raw scraps. See [Food](food.md) and Antiquity's [Cooking & Food](../antiquity/cooking-and-food.md).

=== "Comfort"

    | Thought | Mood |
    | --- | --- |
    | "I love my house!" | Big lift |
    | "Nice housing." / "I like my house." | Small lift |
    | "My house is uncomfortable." | Moderate hit |
    | "I hate my house!" | Heavy hit |
    | "I don't have a place to call home." | Severe — and deepens each day |
    | "Slept rough at the outpost." | Small hit |
    | "I'm badly injured!" / "I'm in pain." | Moderate / small hit |
    | "I've been poisoned!" | Heavy hit |
    | "Recently rained." / "Recently stormed." | Tiny lift-of-the-eyebrow / small hit |

    Home quality reads the **appeal** of the house itself. Roomier, prettier homes lift Comfort; cramming beds into a bare box drags it down. See [Housing](housing.md).

=== "Culture"

    | Thought | Mood |
    | --- | --- |
    | "I love it here!" | Big lift |
    | "I like it here." | Small lift |
    | "I feel uncomfortable here." | Moderate hit |
    | "I hate it here!" | Heavy hit |
    | "My child was born recently." | Huge lift (to the parents) |
    | "A new child was born!" | Lift for the whole settlement |
    | "We progressed recently." | Small lift on completing research |
    | "We forsook the gods." | Lingering hit |

    "It here" reads the **beauty of the ground** citizens spend their day on, not their house. Decorate the places people actually stand. See [Culture & Heraldry](culture-and-heraldry.md).

=== "Society"

    | Thought | Mood |
    | --- | --- |
    | "I got promoted recently." | Lift |
    | "I love working in such a lovely workshop." | Small lift |
    | "My workplace is dreary." | Small hit |
    | "I don't feel like I'm helping." | Deepens while unemployed |
    | "Had a great conversation with …" | Small lift |
    | "Had an argument with …" | Small hit |
    | "Had a fight with …" | Moderate hit |
    | "… died recently." | Hit — heavier the closer the bond |

    Give everyone a job, keep workshops pleasant, and let people socialise. Grief scales with the relationship — losing family or a friend for life cuts deepest (see [Relationships](#relationships-friends-arguments-and-grief)).

!!! info "Crises, policies, and war leave their mark too"
    A running [crisis](crises.md) plants a settlement-wide gloom that clears — with a small rebound of relief — once you resolve it. [Government policies](government.md) add their own thoughts: night-shift and night-watch workers grow weary, adopted settlement pets cheer everyone, wartime speeches steel or tire the people. These come and go with the policy or the war.

## Skills: from Novice to Master

Workers get better at their trade by *doing* it. Every job tracks its own experience, and as it climbs a citizen advances through five tiers:

<div class="bb-skill-ladder" markdown>

**Novice** :material-arrow-right: **Apprentice** :material-arrow-right: **Journeyman** :material-arrow-right: **Veteran** :material-arrow-right: **Master**

</div>

The **Job** tab shows the current tier and a progress bar toward the next. Skill matters most for crafters: a Novice turns out crude, wildly inconsistent goods, while a **Master** reliably produces the finest, most consistent work in the settlement. As a crafter climbs, their average quality rises *and* steadies — and only your most seasoned hands, the top tiers, ever roll a true **masterwork**. Crossing into a new tier fires a short, proud "I got promoted recently." thought.

Two things speed the climb: keeping the worker **Content** (the +15% experience bonus) and simply giving them steady work to do.

!!! warning "Re-skilling resets the clock"
    A citizen's experience is **tied to the specific job they hold**. Move a Veteran forester over to fishing — whether by hand or by the labour balancer shuffling workers around — and they start their *fishing* skill fresh, keeping their forestry experience only if they return to it. Reassign thoughtfully: churning workers between jobs keeps everyone perpetually unskilled. See [Jobs & Labor](jobs-and-labor.md) and [Workshops](workshops.md).

## Stamina: work drains, rest restores

Work is tiring. Every unit of labour spends a little **stamina** from a citizen's pool, and when it runs dry they stop and rest rather than pushing on. On the Job tab you'll see the status **Resting — no stamina** (or **Exhausted** on the summary); the worker steps away, patrols or idles a while, and returns once stamina recharges.

You rarely need to manage this directly — it self-corrects after a short break — but it explains a common sight: a busy worker who keeps pausing mid-task isn't broken, they're catching their breath. Citizens also sleep at night, which is when the settlement resets and most rest happens.

!!! tip "Don't over-stack one worker"
    If a single forester can't keep up with a hungry sawpit, the answer is usually a *second* forester, not staring at the first. Stamina caps how much any one pair of hands can do in a stretch.

## Health & injury

The summary line shows each citizen's **Health**. Barbarian raids, brawls, poison, and plain bad luck can leave a citizen hurt, and it shows up as a thought: **"I'm in pain."** for a scrape, **"I'm badly injured!"** for something serious, or **"I've been poisoned!"** while a toxin runs its course. All three drag on Comfort and, like real people, an injured worker is a slower worker.

Citizens recover over time when they're safe, fed, and rested — so the best first-aid is a defended settlement with food in the stores. Persistent injuries usually mean a threat you haven't dealt with: keep [walls](walls.md) up and [guards](jobs-and-labor.md) posted, and watch for [barbarians](barbarians.md).

## Compliance & resentment: will they even obey?

Happiness is how a citizen *feels*. **Compliance** is whether they'll *listen*. It answers a simpler question: when you assign a job or issue an order, will this person go along with it?

New arrivals show up eager and fully compliant. But **living in misery erodes compliance**, and there is a second, sharper channel: **resentment** toward whoever leads them. A citizen left unhappy under a leader starts to blame that leader personally — and the two feed each other.

!!! warning "Raising a hand against your own people backfires"
    Resentment isn't only earned by neglect — **violence is the fastest way of all to earn it**. Every time you strike a citizen, their resentment toward you climbs by **10** (this holds even for a leader "disciplining" their own people — hitting a citizen is a social act, not the world hurting them). Worse, **killing** a citizen scars the whole settlement: every other citizen gains **40** resentment toward the killer — enough, from a single death, to shove witnesses past the point where compliance starts to crumble. Culling or "managing" citizens by hand is a direct march toward strikes and coups.

!!! danger "Homelessness is the classic trigger"
    A citizen with no home doesn't just sit at a mild penalty. Their grievance **deepens each day**, and the game will warn you plainly:

    > *"3 citizen(s) still have no home — their resentment deepens each day."*

    Build housing *before* you invite more mouths, not after. Chronic homelessness is the single most reliable way to march a happy settlement toward open revolt.

### From grumbling to strikes to coups

Unrest escalates in stages, and dusk is when it comes to a head. As night falls you may see a warning banner:

> *"As night falls, unrest stirs in the settlement:"*

- **Strikes.** *"2 worker(s) are unhappy enough to refuse work tomorrow."* The next day those citizens show **On strike today** / **Refusing to work** and simply won't do their jobs — no amount of assigning fixes it until the mood recovers. Low-compliance workers may also flatly **refuse a job switch** when you ask.
- **Coups.** When more than **45% of a Chiefdom's citizens** carry deep **resentment** toward the Chief, you'll see *"The people may rise against the Chief at dawn!"* What actually happens at dawn depends on how many players are online:
    - **With two or more players online**, the people act — *"The citizens have lost faith in [chief]. They will choose a new leader."* — and hold a vote that hands the seat to another member: *"The citizens have deposed [chief]. [name] now leads."*
    - **Solo, or with only one player online**, there is nobody to elect in your place, so the coup is **suppressed**: you keep your seat, but the anger doesn't just evaporate — **compliance now decays twice as fast**, so strikes and flat refusals pile up sharply until you turn the mood around. The dusk warning still fires either way, so a lone Chief will read the "may rise against the Chief" banner even though no rival ever takes the throne.

The remedy is the same at every stage: **fix the underlying misery** — food, homes, jobs, safety — and give it a day or two. A settlement that feels heard forgives quickly; content citizens shed old grudges fast. Your form of [government](government.md) shapes how forgiving (or fragile) that balance is.

!!! tip "Read the dusk warning as a countdown"
    The nightfall banner is your grace period. If you see strike or coup warnings, you have until morning to visibly improve conditions — hand out a meal, finish a house, resolve a crisis. Ignore it and you'll wake up to a walkout, or a new chief.

## Children and growing up

A settlement grows its own people. Given the right conditions, two citizens will start a family on their own — and every new mouth makes food planning matter more.

**A child begins overnight, and it takes a real couple to make one.** For a pregnancy to catch, an **adult man and adult woman who share the same [home](housing.md)** must both be **asleep in it at night**, and they can't be **family**. Given that, the game rolls their chance once per night, set by how close the two are:

| Their relationship | Chance each shared night |
| --- | --- |
| **Strangers** | None on their own |
| **Acquaintances** | ~15% |
| **Friends** | ~30% |
| **Close Friends** | ~65% |
| **Friends for Life** | ~100% |

The **[home's own Happiness](housing.md)** then nudges every one of those odds up or down. A delightful home can lift even near-strangers over the line and let them conceive; a miserable or overcrowded one drags the chance toward nothing and can stop births outright. So the surest way to grow your own population is warm homes shared by citizens who already like each other — the relationship [ladder below](#relationships-friends-arguments-and-grief) is what your breeding rate rides on.

- **Expecting.** A pregnant citizen shows **Pregnant** on their screen and **Expecting — not working** as their status. The **Info** tab adds a pink "how far along" bar that fills over the pregnancy's roughly **one in-game day** course, and a small marker appears beside her name tag — so you can spot an expecting mother at a glance and plan for the temporary dip in hands.
- **Birth.** A new arrival is announced to everyone — *"[parent] gave birth to [child]!"* — and lifts spirits settlement-wide, with an especially big glow for the proud parents.
- **Childhood.** Children can't do a job. They wander, explore, and think small curious thoughts — *"I saw a bird!"*, *"I found a really cool pebble."*, *"Mom said not to stray far. But my curiosity is killing me!"* — each a little lift to those around them. **You cannot assign a child to a workstation**; the option is simply unavailable until they grow.
- **Growing up.** After roughly **three in-game days**, a child comes of age — *"[name] has grown up."* — and becomes a full adult who can hold a job, take a home, and eventually raise a family of their own.

!!! note "Plan for the next generation"
    Homegrown citizens are free population that arrives loyal — but they're a delayed investment. They eat while they grow and can't work until they mature, so make sure your food and housing can carry the extra head *before* the settlement starts breeding in earnest.

## Relationships: friends, arguments, and grief

Citizens form real bonds. At dusk they gather to **socialise** (you'll see the status **Socializing (dusk)**), and those conversations move relationships up or down. As they talk, a small **speech bubble** hovers over each one showing what's on their mind — a culture, food, or science glyph, a mood face, or their job's workstation icon — an at-a-glance read on what your settlement is chatting and feeling about during the social hour. A good talk leaves both parties happier — *"Had a great conversation with …"* — while a clash leaves an **argument** or, worse, a **fight**. The **Relations** tab lists who a citizen knows and how they feel about each one.

Relationships climb a ladder of tiers as bonds deepen:

**Strangers** :material-arrow-right: **Acquaintances** :material-arrow-right: **Friends** :material-arrow-right: **Close Friends** :material-arrow-right: **Friends for Life**

Parents and children share a permanent **Family** bond that never fades. (Bonds can sour too — repeated fights push a pair down through dislike and rivalry — but most of your settlement drifts toward friendship given time to talk.)

Bonds carry weight when someone dies. A survivor grieves in proportion to the relationship they've lost:

| Who died | Grief |
| --- | --- |
| A member of my family | Deepest |
| My friend for life | Very heavy |
| A close friend | Heavy |
| A friend | Moderate |
| An acquaintance | A pang |

!!! quote "The dusk hour is the social hour"
    Anything that steals the evening — the **night-shift** policy, a running crisis, a raid at dusk — also steals the conversations that build friendships and mood. A settlement that never gets to talk is a lonelier, unhappier one, even when it's productive.

## Death

Citizens can die — to raiders, to hostile mobs, to poison, to the settlement's own crises. A death is felt across the community through the grief thoughts above, and it can echo into unrest if it lands on a settlement already teetering. It also costs you what that citizen was: their skill, their labour, their place in a family.

Your defences are your best population policy. Keep [walls](walls.md) standing and [guards](jobs-and-labor.md) posted, resolve [crises](crises.md) before they turn deadly, and keep the granary full — a safe, fed settlement loses far fewer people, and grows more of its own.
