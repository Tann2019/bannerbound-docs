# Barbarians & Raids

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Beyond your border lie other peoples — some who come to trade, some who come to be fed, and some who come only to take. You can talk to nearly all of them. Whether the conversation ends in a bargain or a burning field is largely up to you.</em></p>

!!! abstract "In short"
    - The wild holds four kinds of camp — **Nomads**, the **Tribe**, **Raiders**, and **Marauders** ("Barbarians") — each with its own temper, set by the land it sits in.
    - Wander near one and it **discovers** your settlement; from then on it sends **envoys** to your town hall. **Right-click an envoy to parley.**
    - At parley you can **pay tribute**, **trade** what they ask for, or **refuse** — and open a **barter** screen where every good has a **worth**. They deal once your offer's worth meets their asking value.
    - Anger a camp — or meet a Marauder — and it **raids** you. **Guards** hold the border; the **Night Watch** policy keeps them there all night.
    - **Repel** a raid and they return in greater force. To end a camp for good, march on it: **slay its commanders and raze its standard.**

## The four camps

A camp's nature is decided by the biome it seeds in. A desert band trades; a jungle tribe demands to be fed; a cold-country warband comes for your herds; and the temperate plains breed the Marauders who simply want tribute. You can tell them apart at a glance — each camp's members wear their name in their own colour.

=== ":material-cactus: Nomads"

    **Traders of the dunes.** Nomads seed in hot country — deserts, badlands, savanna — and they are the friendly face of the wilds. They arrive **neutral** and, unlike the others, they **make no demands**: they come to barter. *"Greetings, settler. We are traders of the dunes — what will you barter today?"*

    Trade with them and relations warm; ignore them and their goodwill slowly cools. Their name-tags and standard are **yellow**.

    - Opening stance: **Neutral**
    - Wants: to **trade** (they'll swap wheat for leather, leather for string, and the like)
    - Can be talked all the way up to **Friendly**

=== ":material-tree: The Tribe"

    **Keepers of the jungle.** The Tribe seeds in jungle, swamp, and mangrove, and it starts out **hostile**. *"The jungle is ours. Feed us, and we may let your people be."* What it wants is **food** — a haunch of cooked meat will buy peace.

    You can placate a Tribe and win back a wary truce, but no further: the best you'll reach is **neutral**. Their colour is **green**.

    - Opening stance: **Hostile**
    - Demands: **food** (cooked pork and the like)
    - Can be calmed back up to **Neutral** — never Friendly

=== ":material-cow: Raiders"

    **The takers of herds.** Raiders seed in cold taiga and demand **meat and livestock**. *"Your herds are fat. Hand over meat, or we will come and take it — and more."* They begin **neutral**, but they are the hardest camp to fight: they field more warriors than any other, march in the largest warbands, strike more often, and are led by **two commanders** rather than one.

    Keep them fed and they can become genuine **allies**; starve them and they will bleed your pastures. Their colour is **light blue**.

    - Opening stance: **Neutral**
    - Demands: **meat** (beef, mutton)
    - Can be won over to **Friendly** — but they hit hardest if you don't

=== ":material-skull: Marauders"

    **The storm at your gates.** In the temperate plains and forests dwell the Marauders — the umbrella's namesake, and the one camp you **cannot** befriend. *"We are the storm at your gates. Pay tribute and live a while longer."* They demand **tribute** (grain, leather), and they are **always hostile**.

    Paying a Marauder buys nothing but time. Their tribute-takers pocket your goods and merely put off the next raid — there is no lasting peace to be had, only a quieter week. Their colour is **red**.

    - Opening stance: **Hostile — permanently**
    - Demands: **tribute**
    - Tribute only **buys time**; the raids always return

| Camp | Home biome | Opening stance | What they want | Best relations reachable |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Nomads** :material-circle:{ style="color:#e5c100" } | Desert / badlands / savanna | Neutral | To trade | Friendly |
| **Tribe** :material-circle:{ style="color:#3bab48" } | Jungle / swamp | Hostile | Food | Neutral |
| **Raiders** :material-circle:{ style="color:#5ca8d8" } | Taiga / cold | Neutral | Meat & herds | Friendly |
| **Marauders** :material-circle:{ style="color:#c8443a" } | Plains / forest | Hostile | Tribute | *None — always hostile* |

!!! tip "The land tells you the temper"
    You can guess a camp's mood before you ever meet it. Crossing a desert? Expect traders. Deep in the jungle? Expect a demand for food. Out on the open plains, brace for Marauders. Claiming and settling flat, temperate ground keeps camps at arm's length — see [Territory](territory.md) — because new camps never seed close to your border.

## Discovering a camp

Camps are already out there before you know it, tucked into unclaimed land well away from your settlement and from one another. You find them the old-fashioned way: by **exploring**. Wander close to a camp with a settlement to your name and it takes notice of you — the chat announces *"%s has discovered %s"* in red, and the camp appears in your Town Hall's **Diplomacy** tab under **Barbarian camps**.

Discovery cuts both ways. Now that the camp knows where you live, it will start sending envoys to your door — and, if its mood sours, raiding parties. But now that *you* know where *it* lives, you can go and end it.

!!! info "The Chronicle points the way"
    While you're near a camp that's hostile to you, a **quest entry** appears in your journal with a waypoint and a compass bearing toward it — *"Reach &lt;camp&gt;"* — alongside the two objectives that clear it: **"Slay the commanders"** and **"Raze the standard."** The entry fades when you travel far away, but your progress is kept. See [the Chronicle](../reference/codex-and-ponder.md) for how journal quests work.

## The envoy at your gate

A camp that has discovered you sends a lone **envoy** — a messenger who travels overland to your town hall (or your public banner if you've no hall yet). You'll meet them on the road if you happen to be near, or find them waiting at your hall when they arrive:

> *An envoy from &lt;camp&gt; has arrived at &lt;settlement&gt;. Right-click them to parley.*

The first envoy comes a short while after discovery, and more follow from time to time. An envoy that goes ignored waits a few minutes at your hall and then simply leaves — no harm done. Only a member of the settlement the envoy was sent to can speak with it.

!!! danger "Never cut down an envoy"
    An envoy is a diplomat, not a target. Kill one — on the road or at your gate — and the camp takes it as the hardest possible refusal: relations plummet and the next raid comes sooner. If you don't want to deal, just walk away and let the messenger leave.

### Right-click to parley

Right-click the envoy to open the **parley** screen. What you see depends on the camp: **Nomads** open straight into a trade, while the three demanding camps first tell you plainly what they want — *"They demand:"* — and offer you a choice.

| Choice | What it does | For which camps |
|---|---|---|
| :material-hand-coin: **Pay tribute (improve relations)** | Hand over what they ask. Your standing rises toward that camp's ceiling and the next raid is pushed back. | Tribe, Raiders — the camps whose mood you can lift |
| :material-timer-sand: **Pay tribute (buy time)** | Hand over tribute knowing it only delays the storm. No real peace — just a quieter stretch. | Marauders |
| :material-swap-horizontal: **Trade %s → %s** | Swap goods at their rate. A little goodwill each time. | Nomads (other camps deal through the barter screen) |
| :material-close-octagon: **Refuse** | Turn them away. Relations worsen, and if that tips them hostile, expect a raid. | Any demanding camp |
| :material-comment-remove: **Say nothing** | Let the envoy leave without answering. No cost, no gain. | Any |

What a camp asks for isn't fixed. Their demands and trade rates **scale to your prosperity** — a large, land-rich settlement reads as wealthy, so camps press for more — and to **your standing** with them. A camp that dislikes you drives a hard bargain; a friendly one eases off and cuts you a better rate.

### The barter screen

Behind every parley is the **barter** table, where you build an offer good by good. It's laid out as two ledgers:

- **Your offer** and **Their offer** — the goods on the table between you.
- **Storage** — what your **settlement stockpile** holds. This is the crucial rule: you pay from your town's stores, *not* from your own pockets. Anything you offer is withdrawn from settlement storage, and anything you receive is handed to you.
- **Their storage** — the goods the camp has to trade.

The whole system runs on one idea: **worth**.

> *Every good is valued by the camp. Hover an item to see its worth.*
> *They accept once your offer's worth meets their asking value.*

Because a deal is judged purely on total worth, you're never locked into paying with the exact items they suggested. Their opening ask is only a **suggestion** — you can build a **counteroffer** out of whatever your stockpile holds, and as long as its combined worth clears the bar, the deal stands. Pile on a little **extra** worth and they warm to you faster; a generous bargain earns more goodwill than a bare-minimum one.

!!! example "Reading a barter"
    A Raider envoy asks for meat your pens can't spare. You open the barter, hover a few items to check their worth, and swap the demand for a stack of leather and a basket of grain from storage instead — different goods, same worth. The **Deal balance** ticks over to **Fair deal**, you propose it, and *"The deal is struck."* Your standing with the Raiders climbs a notch, and their next warband is a while off.

### Verdicts

When you propose an offer, the camp judges it on the spot:

| Verdict | Meaning | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| :material-check: **Acceptable** | Your offer's worth met their asking value. | The deal is struck; goods change hands; relations improve (more so if you overpaid). |
| :material-alert: **Unacceptable** | A lowball — the worth fell short. | *"Relationships will worsen."* They leave insulted, and your standing drops. |
| :material-package-variant-closed-remove: **Your storage can't back this** | You offered goods your stockpile doesn't actually hold. | Nothing changes — fix the offer and try again. |

The running **Deal balance** tells you where you stand before you commit: **Fair deal**, **Need %s more worth**, or **Not enough in storage**. Watch that line and you'll never insult a camp by accident.

!!! warning "Fill your stockpile before you negotiate"
    Barter draws from settlement storage, so a deal is only as good as what your town actually has on hand. If your granaries and tanneries are empty, even a willing camp has nothing to accept. Keep a reserve of trade staples — grain, leather, cooked meat — in reach of your town hall before an envoy arrives. See [Food](food.md) and the [Tannery](../antiquity/tannery.md) for where those goods come from.

### The grace period

Sometimes a camp wants something you don't yet have. A camp you're on decent terms with may extend you **credit** — choose **Get it for you** and, if they're willing, they grant you time:

> *&lt;camp&gt; grants you two days. "I'll be back. You'd better have it."*

A collector envoy returns when the debt comes due, opening a barter for the goods you promised. Gather them in the meantime and you settle up cleanly.

Hostile camps have no patience for this. Ask a camp that already despises you — or any Marauder — to wait, and the answer is flat: *"No. We want it now."*

## Relations: hostile, neutral, friendly

Every camp holds a private opinion of your settlement, shown in the Diplomacy tab as one of three stances. Where you sit decides whether envoys come to trade or warbands come to burn.

=== ":material-emoticon-angry: Hostile"

    The camp wants you gone. Hostile camps **schedule raids** against you and drive hard bargains at parley. Every demanding camp except the Marauders can be **lifted out** of hostility by paying tribute and trading fairly — but Marauders live here permanently, and no amount of tribute moves them.

=== ":material-emoticon-neutral: Neutral"

    An uneasy coexistence. Neutral camps won't raid you, but they'll keep sending envoys with their demands, and a few refusals will sour them back toward hostility. The Tribe's ceiling is here — placate them and they'll leave you be, but they'll never be friends.

=== ":material-emoticon-happy: Friendly"

    A genuine ally. Friendly camps cut you the best trade rates, ease their demands, and leave your borders in peace. Only **Nomads** and **Raiders** can be won this far — and it takes a steady history of fair dealing to get there.

Your standing moves with every interaction, and each shift is announced — *"Relations with &lt;camp&gt; improve."* in green, or *"...worsen."* in red:

| This raises your standing | This lowers it |
|---|---|
| Paying tribute to a camp that can be won over | Refusing a demand |
| Trading, especially generously | Lowballing a barter |
| Clearing a barter with worth to spare | Killing an envoy *(the sharpest drop of all)* |
| | Ignoring a demanding camp's needs over time |

!!! tip "Turn a threat into a supply line"
    A Raider camp is the classic example. Fed steadily, they climb from a menace on your northern edge to a **friendly** trading partner with the best rates you'll find — and you never have to fight the toughest warband in the wilds. Diplomacy is nearly always cheaper than defence.

## Raids

When a hostile camp's patience runs out — because you refused it, cut down its envoy, or simply because it's a Marauder — it musters a **raid**. A warband spawns in a ring around your settlement and marches inward on your town hall. The alarm is unmistakable:

> *%s from %s are raiding your settlement!*

A **war-horn** sounds, and a banner locks to the top of your screen for the whole assault:

> **Your settlement is being RAIDED**

Raiders strike more often than any other camp; Marauders come less frequently but hit as part of their whole demand-or-die cycle. You cannot disband a settlement mid-raid — *"survive the raid or make peace first."*

### Holding the line

Raids are fought by your **Guards**. A guard is a trained watch — far steadier in a brawl than an ordinary citizen, patrolling your borders and refusing to be lured off into the wild. When a raid lands, guards converge on the threat and hold the line while it plays out. Unlock the Guard through research and post a few before you're ever discovered; see [Jobs & Labor](jobs-and-labor.md) for how to assign one.

Timing matters. A raid that comes at **night** can catch a sleeping watch:

> *Your watch was asleep in their beds — the horn wakes them, but the Night Watch policy keeps guards on the border all night.*

The **Night Watch** policy is the answer. It keeps your guards awake and patrolling the border until dawn instead of sleeping — at a cost of **−12 happiness** to those guards. If you live anywhere near a hostile camp, it's often worth the grumbling. Enact it from the Policies tab; see [Government & Law](government.md) for how policies work.

!!! tip "Layer your defences"
    Guards are your spearhead, but they fight better behind help. **[Walls](walls.md) and gates** funnel a warband into a killing lane instead of letting it swarm your hall. **Poison blowdarts** from your [herbalists](../antiquity/herbalism.md) turn a defender's volley lethal, and trained [archers](../antiquity/fletching-and-archery.md) thin a raid before it closes. And you can start the fight on your terms — a projectile loosed into a camp's own ground rouses its warriors to come to *you*.

!!! note "Rallying your people"
    While a raid is on — or during open war — you can **rally** your citizens to fight alongside the guards. Ordinary settlers make poor soldiers, but numbers count when the wall is breached. See [Diplomacy](diplomacy.md) for rallying and the wider rules of war.

### Repelling a raid — and the cost of winning

There are two ways a raid ends. If you **cut down the entire warband**, you repel it:

> *Raid repelled — %s has been driven back, and will return in greater force.*

Read that last clause carefully. Every raid you crush makes the camp angrier and better prepared: the **next** warband arrives **larger and sooner**. Repelling raids is survival, not victory — it buys you the field today while the threat compounds. If a camp is raiding you on repeat, beating back wave after wave is a losing race.

The other ending is quieter. If you can't finish the squad, its survivors give up after a few minutes and pull back:

> *The raiders from %s have withdrawn.*

Either way the RAIDED banner drops and the horn falls silent — but the camp still stands, and it will come again.

!!! warning "You can't defend your way to peace"
    Because repelled raids escalate, sitting behind your walls forever only sharpens the sword aimed at you. Against a camp you can befriend, **buy peace at parley**. Against a Marauder — who can never be befriended and whose tribute only delays the inevitable — the only permanent answer is to march out and **clear the camp**.

## Clearing a camp for good

To be rid of a camp forever, you take the fight to it. Two things must happen, and the Chronicle tracks both:

- [ ] **Slay the commanders.** Each camp is led by crowned commanders — one for most camps, **two** for a Raider camp. Killed commanders never return.
- [ ] **Raze the standard.** Break the camp's central banner. Razing the standard stops the ordinary warriors from respawning, so you can mop up whoever's left.

Do both and the camp is finished:

> *%s has been cleared — its standard razed and its commanders slain.*

The order that works is usually **standard first, then commanders** — once the banner falls, no fresh warriors replace the ones you cut down, and the commanders are all that remain between you and a cleared field.

!!! success "A razing is permanent"
    A cleared camp is gone for good — the ground it stood on never breeds another. Razing the toughest camp near your border is a real, lasting win: one fewer source of raids, forever. It's hard-won, but unlike repelling raids, it actually *ends* the threat.

!!! example "Ending a Marauder camp"
    You can't befriend the plains Marauders, and their tribute only delays them — so you settle it. You find the red camp from the waypoint in your Chronicle, loose an arrow into its ground to draw the warriors out and thin them in the open, then push in and **break the standard**. With respawns stopped, you hunt down the crowned commander, and the chat rewards you: *the camp has been cleared.* The plains to your west are quiet at last.
