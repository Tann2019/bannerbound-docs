# Government & Law

<p class="bb-lead"><em>Every tribe begins without a ruler. How you choose to govern — a single decisive Chief, or a council that decides together — shapes who gives the orders, how your people feel about them, and which doctrines you can adopt.</em></p>

!!! abstract "In short"
    - Before you enact a **code of laws**, your settlement is in **anarchy**: citizens self-organize, but they *won't obey* direct job assignments — you can only ask them to switch jobs or set drop-offs.
    - When the tribe is established, everyone votes on a government: a **Chiefdom** (one Chief) or a **Council** (needs multiple players).
    - A **Chief** holds exclusive powers — directing research, assigning jobs, changing policies, negotiating trade, and issuing orders — and can be **deposed by a coup** if unrest runs high.
    - A **Council** settles weighty matters by **majority vote** in chat.
    - **Policies** occupy limited slots and grant lasting perks — but almost every one carries a cost.

## Anarchy: the leaderless baseline

Before you adopt a government, your settlement lives under **anarchy**. This is not a punishment or a failure state — it is the self-organizing baseline that keeps a young tribe fed while you decide how to rule.

Your citizens are neither idle nor hostile. They **auto-employ** into whichever basic gatherer jobs you have unlocked, working tool-free and more slowly than a directed worker would. A leaderless band still gathers, forages, and fishes.

What anarchy *can't* do is take orders. Without a code of laws, no one holds the authority to command labor:

- **You cannot pin citizens to workstations.** Try to assign a job at a workstation and you'll be told *"No code of laws — citizens won't obey assignments yet."*
- **You can only ask.** Open a citizen and use **Request switch** to ask them to change jobs — and they *may refuse*. If they just turned you down, give them a minute before asking again.
- **You can nudge their work with drop-offs.** Marking a **drop-off** chest steers where self-organized gatherers deposit what they bring in.

!!! warning "Anarchy has no authority at all"
    Exile, direct research, trade negotiation, and job pinning are all locked in anarchy — *"No one holds the authority to exile in anarchy. Form a government."* If your camp feels ungovernable, that's the point: it's time to enact laws.

See [Citizens](citizens.md) and [Jobs & Labor](jobs-and-labor.md) for how requesting, drop-offs, and pinning work once you have a government.

## Enacting the code of laws

Once your tribe is properly established, a prompt announces that **it is time to enact the code of laws**. This is the moment your settlement leaves anarchy behind and chooses how it will be governed.

The choice is put to a vote: a clickable prompt appears in chat, everyone online casts a ballot, and **the majority wins**. There are two early forms of government, and the one you pick is more than flavor — it gates which culture doctrines and policies you can later adopt.

=== "Chiefdom"

    A **Chiefdom** concentrates power in one person. The **Chief** assigns jobs directly, directs research, changes policies, and personally resolves the suggestions raised by citizens. It is fast and direct — well suited to a small, focused settlement or a solo game.

    - :material-account-star: One leader makes the calls
    - :material-flash: Decisions happen instantly
    - :material-lock: Unlocks Chiefdom-only doctrines and its signature policy, **Workload Share**

=== "Council"

    A **Council** spreads power across the online players. Major decisions become clickable **chat-votes** that a majority settles, and citizens feel genuinely consulted. It trades speed for legitimacy — nothing weighty happens instantly, but a united tribe pulls harder.

    !!! note "Multiple players required"
        A Council needs more than one player in the settlement. Solo, only the Chiefdom is available.

    - :material-account-group: Online players decide together
    - :material-scale-balance: Consensus lifts morale
    - :material-lock: Unlocks Council-only doctrines and its signature policy, **Opinionated Crowd**

| | Chiefdom | Council |
|---|---|---|
| **Who decides** | A single elected Chief | Online players, by majority vote |
| **Players needed** | Works solo | Two or more |
| **Speed** | Instant | Runs on time-boxed votes |
| **How citizens feel** | Direct rule; overuse wears on morale | Consulted; consensus boosts spirits |
| **Signature policy** | Workload Share | Opinionated Crowd |

## The Chiefdom in detail

### Electing a Chief

When a Chiefdom is declared, a **Chief election** opens: *"Click a member to nominate them."* Every member casts a nomination for the settler they want to lead, and the most-favored candidate is seated. You can change your nomination while the window is open. When it closes, the settlement announces its new leader — *"%s has been elected Chief!"*

### The Chief's exclusive powers

The Chief is the only person who may act on the settlement's behalf. Everyone else can *suggest*, but only the Chief can *do*.

| Power | What only the Chief may do |
|---|---|
| :material-flask: **Direct research** | Choose the next science or culture node. Members can only suggest a node. |
| :material-account-hard-hat: **Assign jobs** | Pin citizens to workstations and hand-assign labor. |
| :material-tune: **Change policies** | Enact or remove policies (subject to a vote — see below). |
| :material-swap-horizontal: **Negotiate trade** | Strike deals with city-states and neighbors. |
| :material-bullhorn: **Issue orders** | Exile a citizen, issue registration tablets and paper, and command the settlement generally. |

Attempt any of these as an ordinary member and the town hall turns you away — *"Only the Chief may issue orders."* Reach for a research node and it points you to the alternative instead: *"Only the Chief may direct research. Click a node to suggest it instead."*

!!! tip "The Chief is a bottleneck by design"
    Direct power is the Chiefdom's whole strength — but lean on it too hard and your people stop feeling heard. Resolve the occasional suggestion and let the tribe win a few decisions, or resentment builds toward a coup.

### Stepping down and regents

A Chief who wants out opens the town hall and chooses **Step Down as Chief**. There's a floor on this: you must **lead for at least 20 minutes** before you're allowed to abdicate (*"You must lead for at least 20 minutes before stepping down"*). Stepping down throws the seat open and a new leader must be chosen.

When the Chief simply logs off, the settlement doesn't grind to a halt. A **regent** steps in to fill the role while the Chief is away — *"%s is filling in as regent while the Chief is away"* — and steps back down when the Chief returns.

### Coups

A Chief rules only as long as the people tolerate them. Let **unrest** climb high enough and the settlement will turn. First a warning: *"The people may rise against the Chief at dawn!"* If nothing changes, the citizens depose the Chief at first light and install a new leader — *"The citizens have deposed %s. %s now leads."*

!!! danger "Read the warnings"
    A coup fires at **dawn**, not immediately — so a coup warning is a countdown, not a verdict. Address the unhappiness driving it ([food](food.md), [housing](housing.md), overwork, ignored suggestions) before the sun rises and you keep your seat. See [Crises](crises.md) for what pushes a settlement to the brink.

## The Council in detail

A Council has no single seat of power. Instead, the weighty matters that a Chief would decide alone are put to the settlement as a **vote**. When a decision is called, a clickable prompt appears in chat; online players click **[Yes]** or **[No]**, and a majority within the time limit carries it.

Your vote is final once cast — *"Your vote is final. Waiting for the others."* The town hall's **Votes** tab lists what's currently being decided and how recent votes went, so nobody misses a ballot.

Council government rewards bringing people with you: when citizens feel consulted, they pull together. The cost is that nothing momentous happens the instant you want it to.

## Votes

Votes are how a settlement settles its heaviest choices without a lone ruler's word. Each one runs for a set **time window**, then passes, is rejected, or expires. You'll find active and recent votes on the town hall's **Votes** tab, and you cast yours from the clickable chat prompt or the tab itself.

| Vote | What it decides |
|---|---|
| **Exile** | Cast out a troublesome citizen. |
| **Issue registration tablet** | Produce a tablet to admit a new member. |
| **Issue registration paper** | Produce a registration paper. |
| **Declare war** | Open hostilities with another settlement. |
| **Offer peace** | Extend a peace offer to an enemy. |
| **Toggle Rally Citizens** | Turn your defenders' rally on or off. |
| **Raze** | Level a captured target. |
| **Accept a trade** | Put an offered trade to a quick tribe-wide vote. |

!!! note "Votes aren't only for Councils"
    Even under a Chiefdom, weighty acts like exile or war can be routed through a vote instead of resting on one leader's shoulders. And policy and palette changes always pass through a vote of their own before they take effect.

## Suggestions

A Chiefdom still hears its people. Members raise **Suggestions** on the town hall's **Suggestions** tab, and an unread suggestion is flagged so the Chief doesn't miss it. Citizens can suggest:

- **Research** — a science or culture node to pursue next
- **A policy** to enact
- **A palette** to adopt (see [Culture & Heraldry](culture-and-heraldry.md))
- **Exile** of a specific citizen
- Issuing a **registration tablet** or **paper**

For each one, the Chief chooses **Resolve** — acting on what the citizens want — or **Ignore** it. Resolving keeps your people content; ignoring saves effort but slowly wears on morale (*"The chief has chosen to ignore your suggestion..."*).

!!! tip "Suggestions are your morale dial"
    You'll never please everyone, but resolving suggestions is the cheapest way to keep a Chiefdom's people feeling heard. Treat the Suggestions tab as an early-warning system: a stack of ignored requests is exactly the unrest that ferments into a coup.

During a [crisis](crises.md), advisors can go a step further and directly **advise the Chief** on how to respond — *"You advised the chief to: ..."*

## Policies

**Policies** are standing rules that reshape how your settlement works — longer work hours, faster roads, guarded nights. You manage them on the town hall's **Policies** tab.

### How policies work

- **Research unlocks them.** A policy only becomes available once a completed science *or* culture node opens it. Until then the tab reads *"Research unlocks policies."*
- **Slots are limited.** Your active policies fill a fixed set of **slots** — each policy fits a slot of its matching **type**. To adopt a new one when you're full, you must first remove another (*"No free policy slot — remove one first"*).
- **The Chief enacts them, with a vote.** Only the Chief may change policies, and each change is put to a policy vote before it takes hold. Members who aren't Chief can only **Suggest** a policy.
- **Almost every policy has a cost.** Most trade a real benefit against a happiness or compliance hit — read the effect line before you commit.

### Policy types and the signature slot

Every policy is tagged with one type — **Economic**, **Cultural**, **Scientific**, **Militaristic**, **Diplomatic**, or **Faith** — and it fills a slot of that matching type. **Diplomatic** and **Faith** slots only ever appear once a research or system unlocks them, so you won't see them sitting empty in the early game.

On top of those typed slots, each government keeps a single **signature slot** reserved for its one exclusive policy — **Workload Share** for a Chiefdom, **Opinionated Crowd** for a Council. You can only run your own government's signature policy.

### Known policies

| Policy | Type | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Extended Shift** | Economic | Citizens keep working past dusk and skip socializing before bed. *Working citizens: −10 happiness.* |
| **Workload Share** :material-star:{ title="Chiefdom signature" } | Economic (Chiefdom signature) | Any settlement member can assign citizens to jobs, not just the Chief. *All citizens: −10 compliance while active.* |
| **Opinionated Crowd** :material-star:{ title="Council signature" } | Cultural (Council signature) | Major votes also poll the whole tribe. *On agreement: +25 compliance and −10 resentment for 10 minutes.* |
| **Domestication** | Cultural | Citizens adopt wandering wolves as settlement pets. *All citizens: +15 happiness, −15 compliance while active.* |
| **Agricultural Effort** | Economic | Cultivated farmland yields double food — *but farmers stop tilling new ground.* |
| **Roads** | Cultural | Citizens prefer dirt paths and move faster on them. *+15% speed on paths; requires the Paving science.* |
| **Rallying Speeches** | Militaristic | Wartime speeches hold the settlement together. *While at war: +10 compliance, −10 happiness.* Exclusive with Glory Tales. |
| **Glory Tales** | Cultural | New wars become stories of shared honor before fear sets in. *Once per enemy war: +10 happiness and +20 compliance for 1 day.* Exclusive with Rallying Speeches. |
| **Prospecting Quarry** | Scientific | Quarry crews watch the rubble for ore. *Quarryworkers have a small chance to recover common raw ore from natural stone.* |
| **Night Watch** | Militaristic | The watch stands through the night, skipping sleep to guard the border until dawn. *Guards: −12 happiness.* |

!!! warning "Rallying Speeches and Glory Tales don't mix"
    These two are **mutually exclusive** — you can run one or the other, never both. Rallying Speeches is a steady wartime bonus at a happiness cost; Glory Tales is a one-time morale surge at the start of each new war. Pick the one that fits how you fight.

!!! example "A wartime policy loadout"
    Facing a neighbor's warband, a Chief might enact **Night Watch** so guards hold the border overnight, then choose between **Rallying Speeches** (sustained compliance for a long siege) or **Glory Tales** (a burst of morale to rush an early strike). Both militaristic slots earn their keep only while the war is on — swap them back out once peace returns and the happiness penalties stop.
